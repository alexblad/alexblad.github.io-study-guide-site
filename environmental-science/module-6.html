Global Warming, Climate Change & Greenhouse Gases

What is the difference between climate and weather? Be able to sort statements/descriptions into climate or weather.

  • Weather:
    • **Definition:** Refers to the **short-term, day-to-day** atmospheric conditions at a specific place and time. It's what you experience outside right now or in the next few days.
    • **Examples:** "It's raining today," "The temperature will be $25^\circ C$ tomorrow," "There's a hurricane forming in the Atlantic."
  • Climate:
    • **Definition:** Refers to the **long-term average** of weather patterns in a region, typically over 30 years or more. It describes the typical weather for a given season or location.
    • **Examples:** "The Sahara Desert has an arid climate," "New York experiences a humid continental climate with four distinct seasons," "Global average temperatures are increasing."

**Analogy:** Climate is what you expect (e.g., hot summers in Arizona), weather is what you get (e.g., a specific thunderstorm in Arizona on a particular day).

Explain the terms global warming and climate change.

  • Global Warming:
    • **Definition:** Refers specifically to the **long-term increase in Earth's average surface temperature** due to human activities, primarily the emission of greenhouse gases. It is a component of the broader phenomenon of climate change.
    • **Focus:** Primarily on the temperature aspect of climate.
  • Climate Change:
    • **Definition:** Refers to **long-term shifts in temperatures and weather patterns** globally or regionally. These shifts may be natural, but since the 1800s, human activities have been the main driver of climate change, primarily due to the burning of fossil fuels (coal, oil, and gas), which produces heat-trapping gases.
    • **Focus:** Encompasses not just warming, but also changes in precipitation patterns, sea level rise, increased frequency and intensity of extreme weather events, ocean acidification, and other impacts. Global warming is a key aspect of climate change.

How much warmer is Earth overall (in the past century)? When did the temperature begin to rise drastically? Describe how warming is impacting some regions differently from others. Which regions of the U.S. are experiencing the most drastic increases in temperature? How much warmer is Earth as compared to average (i.e., what is the "temperature anomaly")?

  • **Earth's Warming (Past Century):** Earth's average surface temperature has risen by approximately **$1.1^\circ C$ (2.0$^\circ F$)** since the late 19th century (around 1880). The most significant warming has occurred in the last 40 years, with the five warmest years on record all occurring since 2015.
  • **When Temperature Began to Rise Drastically:** The temperature began to rise drastically from the **1970s onwards**, accelerating significantly in the last few decades of the 20th century and continuing into the 21st century.
  • **How Warming Impacts Regions Differently:** Warming is not uniform across the globe; some regions are warming much faster than others.
    • **Arctic Amplification:** The Arctic region is warming at least two to four times faster than the global average, leading to rapid sea ice melt, permafrost thaw, and impacts on Indigenous communities and wildlife.
    • **Land vs. Ocean:** Land areas generally warm faster than oceans.
    • **Mid-Latitude Extremes:** Some mid-latitude regions experience more frequent and intense heat waves.
    • **Precipitation Changes:** Some areas are experiencing more intense rainfall and flooding, while others face increased drought.
  • **Regions of the U.S. with Most Drastic Temperature Increases:** The **Arctic (Alaska)** is experiencing the most drastic increases in temperature within the U.S. (due to Arctic amplification). Other regions with significant warming trends include the **Southwest U.S.** (leading to more severe droughts and wildfires) and parts of the **Northeast**.
  • **Temperature Anomaly:** A **temperature anomaly** is the difference between the observed temperature and a long-term average (or "normal") temperature for a specific location and time period. It is used to show how much warmer or colder a given period is compared to a baseline. Currently, Earth's global average temperature is about **$1.1^\circ C$ (2.0$^\circ F$) warmer** than the pre-industrial average (1850-1900), representing a significant positive temperature anomaly.

Explain earth’s energy balance, and how greenhouse gases influence the amount of energy retained in the atmosphere. Does it change the overall balance of heat that enters and leaves Earth, or just how long it is retained? How does this cause heating? Also, what happens to energy (radiation) that is absorbed at Earth’s surface? What role does the “natural” greenhouse effect have on making Earth’s climate livable?

  • **Earth's Energy Balance:** Earth's energy balance refers to the equilibrium between the amount of solar radiation (energy) that enters Earth's atmosphere and the amount of energy that is radiated back into space. For Earth's temperature to remain stable, the incoming solar energy must equal the outgoing energy.
  • **How Greenhouse Gases Influence Energy Retention:**
    • Greenhouse gases (GHGs) in the atmosphere (like $CO_2$, methane, water vapor) are transparent to incoming shortwave solar radiation but absorb and re-emit outgoing longwave (infrared) radiation from Earth's surface.
    • As human activities increase the concentration of GHGs, more of the outgoing longwave radiation is absorbed and re-emitted back towards Earth's surface and lower atmosphere. This traps more heat.
  • **Does it change the overall balance or just retention?:** It changes the **overall balance** of heat that enters and leaves Earth. By trapping more outgoing heat, GHGs create an **energy imbalance**, where more energy is retained in the Earth system than is escaping to space. This positive energy imbalance is what causes heating.
  • **How this causes heating:** The increased absorption and re-emission of outgoing infrared radiation by GHGs acts like a blanket, warming the lower atmosphere and the Earth's surface. This continuous trapping of more heat leads to a rise in global average temperatures.
  • **What happens to energy (radiation) absorbed at Earth’s surface?:** When incoming solar radiation is absorbed at Earth's surface (by land, oceans, vegetation), it warms the surface. This warmed surface then re-emits energy back towards the atmosphere as **longwave (infrared) radiation**.
  • **Role of the “Natural” Greenhouse Effect:** The "natural" greenhouse effect is absolutely vital for making Earth's climate livable. Without naturally occurring greenhouse gases (primarily water vapor and $CO_2$), Earth's average temperature would be around $-18^\circ C$ ($0^\circ F$), far too cold to support liquid water and most life as we know it. The natural greenhouse effect keeps Earth warm enough for life to thrive. The problem is the *enhancement* of this natural effect by human-caused emissions.

Discuss which drivers are causing Earth's atmosphere and oceans to warm drastically over the past century.

The drastic warming of Earth's atmosphere and oceans over the past century is overwhelmingly driven by **human activities**, primarily leading to an increase in atmospheric greenhouse gas concentrations. The main drivers include:

  • **Burning of Fossil Fuels:** This is the single largest contributor.
    • **Coal, Oil, and Natural Gas:** When burned for electricity generation, transportation, industrial processes, and heating/cooling, they release large amounts of **carbon dioxide ($CO_2$)** into the atmosphere.
  • **Deforestation and Land-Use Change:**
    • **Deforestation:** Forests act as "carbon sinks" (absorbing $CO_2$). When forests are cut down or burned (e.g., for agriculture or development), stored carbon is released as $CO_2$, and the capacity to absorb future $CO_2$ is reduced.
    • **Other Land-Use Changes:** Conversion of natural ecosystems to agriculture or urban areas can also release greenhouse gases.
  • **Agriculture:**
    • **Methane ($CH_4$):** Released from livestock (enteric fermentation in ruminants like cattle), rice cultivation, and manure management. Methane is a potent greenhouse gas.
    • **Nitrous Oxide ($N_2O$):** Released from the use of synthetic fertilizers and manure management. Nitrous oxide is also a powerful greenhouse gas.
  • **Industrial Processes:** Certain industrial activities (e.g., cement production, chemical manufacturing) release greenhouse gases directly or indirectly.
  • **Waste Management:** Decomposition of organic waste in landfills produces methane.

These anthropogenic emissions enhance the natural greenhouse effect, leading to a net increase in retained heat and thus global warming. Natural factors (like solar variability and volcanic activity) have played minor roles or have had a cooling effect over this period, and cannot explain the observed rapid warming.

Historical Context of Climate Change

Describe the contribution to modern climate science of each of the following scientists/naturalists or teams of scientists:

  • **Jean Baptiste Joseph Fourier:**
    • **Contribution:** In the 1820s, he first proposed the concept of the **"greenhouse effect."** He recognized that Earth's atmosphere somehow trapped heat, preventing it from radiating directly back into space, making the planet warmer than it would otherwise be. He compared it to a "hotbox" (like a greenhouse).
  • **Jean-Pierre Perraudin, Ignace (Ignaz) Venetz, Jean de Charpentier & Louis Agassiz - their role in paleoclimatology:**
    • **Contribution:** These Swiss naturalists and scientists were instrumental in developing the **theory of ice ages** (glacial periods), which is a cornerstone of paleoclimatology (the study of past climates).
      • **Perraudin & Venetz:** Early observers who noted evidence of past extensive glaciation in the Alps, suggesting glaciers had once been much larger.
      • **De Charpentier:** Systematically gathered evidence for past glaciation and influenced Agassiz.
      • **Agassiz:** Popularized and championed the "Ice Age Theory" in the 1830s, presenting compelling evidence of widespread glacial features far from existing glaciers, convincing the scientific community that Earth had experienced periods of extensive ice cover. This fundamentally changed understanding of Earth's climate history.
  • **John Tyndall, Svante Arrhenius, Guy Callendar, Hans Suess & Roger Revelle - how they furthered understanding of the role of CO2 in our climate system:**
    • **John Tyndall (1850s):**
      • **Contribution:** Experimentally demonstrated that certain gases, particularly **water vapor and carbon dioxide ($CO_2$)**, strongly absorb and emit infrared (heat) radiation. This provided the fundamental physical basis for the greenhouse effect, identifying the key atmospheric components responsible for trapping heat.
    • **Svante Arrhenius (1890s):**
      • **Contribution:** Was the first to quantify the relationship between atmospheric $CO_2$ concentrations and Earth's temperature. He calculated that a doubling of $CO_2$ would lead to a significant global temperature increase. He also linked past ice ages to changes in $CO_2$. He essentially made the first prediction of **human-caused global warming** from fossil fuel burning.
    • **Guy Callendar (1930s-1960s):**
      • **Contribution:** Systematically compiled historical weather data and $CO_2$ measurements, showing that both global temperatures and $CO_2$ concentrations were rising. He argued that human emissions of $CO_2$ from fossil fuels were responsible for the observed warming, reviving Arrhenius's ideas.
    • **Hans Suess & Roger Revelle (1950s):**
      • **Contribution:** Suess, a geochemist, used isotopic analysis to show that the increasing $CO_2$ in the atmosphere was indeed coming from the burning of fossil fuels (which have a distinct isotopic signature). Revelle, an oceanographer, recognized that the oceans could not absorb all the excess $CO_2$ being emitted, meaning a significant portion would remain in the atmosphere. Their work highlighted the urgency of measuring atmospheric $CO_2$ precisely and laid the groundwork for the Keeling Curve.
  • **Milutin Milankovitch - his cool cycles!**
    • **Contribution:** A Serbian geophysicist and astronomer who, in the early 20th century, calculated the long-term cyclical variations in Earth's orbit and axial tilt. These variations, known as **Milankovitch Cycles**, influence the amount and distribution of solar radiation reaching Earth's surface over tens to hundreds of thousands of years.
    • **Significance:** His work provided a theoretical framework for understanding the natural drivers of Earth's past ice ages and interglacial periods, demonstrating that long-term climate changes are linked to astronomical cycles.
  • **Charles Keeling & James Hansen - Keeling Curve and Hockey Stick Graph:**
    • **Charles Keeling (1950s-present):**
      • **Contribution:** Initiated continuous, precise measurements of atmospheric $CO_2$ at Mauna Loa Observatory, Hawaii. The resulting data, known as the **"Keeling Curve,"** provides an undeniable, continuous record of the steady increase in atmospheric $CO_2$ concentrations since 1958, along with its annual seasonal cycle. It is one of the most iconic and important pieces of evidence for human-caused climate change.
    • **James Hansen (late 1980s-present):**
      • **Contribution:** A NASA climate scientist who, in 1988, famously testified before the U.S. Congress, stating with high confidence that global warming was underway and that human-caused greenhouse gas emissions were responsible. He and his team developed climate models and produced the **"Hockey Stick Graph"** (though this term is more associated with Michael Mann's work, Hansen's work showed similar rapid warming trends). His work helped bring climate change to significant public and political attention.

Describe how Louis Agassiz' theory was received at the Swiss Society of Natural Science.

Louis Agassiz's theory of a past "Great Ice Age" was initially met with **skepticism and resistance** when he presented it to the Swiss Society of Natural Sciences in 1837. His ideas were radical for the time, challenging the prevailing geological views that attributed features like erratic boulders and carved valleys to the Great Flood (Noah's Ark). Many scientists found it difficult to accept the idea of such a massive, widespread geological force as continental glaciation. However, Agassiz was persistent and gathered overwhelming evidence from his field observations, eventually convincing the scientific community of the validity of his ice age theory. His work ultimately transformed the understanding of Earth's climate history.

Explain why people were not alarmed at Guy Callendar's findings.

People were generally **not alarmed** at Guy Callendar's findings in the mid-20th century (1930s-1960s) for several reasons:

  • **Small Observed Warming:** While Callendar identified a warming trend, the observed temperature increase was still relatively small at that time, and within the range of natural variability that people were accustomed to. It wasn't yet seen as a drastic or immediate threat.
  • **Dominant Concerns:** The mid-20th century was marked by other major global concerns, including World War II, post-war reconstruction, the Cold War, and rapid economic growth. Environmental issues, particularly long-term climate change, were not high on the public or political agenda.
  • **Limited Scientific Consensus & Communication:** While Callendar's work was significant, the broader scientific community had not yet reached the strong consensus on human-caused climate change that exists today. Public understanding of complex atmospheric science was also limited.
  • **Belief in Ocean Absorption:** Many scientists (like Roger Revelle initially) believed that the vast oceans would absorb most of the excess $CO_2$, preventing a significant atmospheric build-up. This belief reduced perceived urgency.
  • **Lack of Direct Evidence:** The continuous, high-precision $CO_2$ measurements (like the Keeling Curve) and sophisticated climate models that would later provide undeniable evidence were not yet available or fully developed.

It took decades of further research, more compelling data, and growing scientific consensus for climate change to become a widespread public and political concern.

Paleoclimatology - Proxy Tools for Past Climate Change

What is paleoclimatology? What is a climate proxy? Describe the climate proxy tools below, how they are used (i.e., what they can tell us) and how far back in time they can provide information about:

  • **Paleoclimatology:** The scientific study of **past climates**. It uses evidence from various natural sources to reconstruct Earth's climate history, often long before direct instrumental records were available.
  • **Climate Proxy:** A **climate proxy** is a natural recorder of past climate conditions. It is preserved physical, chemical, or biological material that can be analyzed to infer past environmental variables like temperature, precipitation, atmospheric composition, or ice volume.
  • **Climate Proxy Tools:**
    • **Ice Cores:**
      • **How used:** Long cylinders of ice drilled from ice sheets (Antarctica, Greenland) or glaciers. Trapped air bubbles provide direct samples of past atmospheric composition ($CO_2$, methane). Isotopic ratios of oxygen and hydrogen in the ice itself reveal past temperatures. Dust, volcanic ash, and pollen layers indicate past environmental conditions.
      • **What they tell us:** Past atmospheric greenhouse gas concentrations, temperatures, precipitation, volcanic activity, atmospheric circulation, and dust levels.
      • **How far back:** Up to **800,000 years** (from Antarctic ice cores), and potentially even further.
    • **Speleothems (Cave Formations):**
      • **How used:** Stalagmites, stalactites, and flowstones formed from mineral-rich water dripping in caves. Their growth rings, isotopic composition (oxygen, carbon), and trace element content reflect past rainfall, temperature, and vegetation changes above the cave.
      • **What they tell us:** Past precipitation patterns (droughts/wet periods), temperature, vegetation type, and fire history.
      • **How far back:** Hundreds of thousands to **millions of years** (depending on the formation).
    • **Ocean/Lake Sediments:**
      • **How used:** Cores drilled from the bottom of oceans or lakes. Layers of sediment contain fossils (e.g., foraminifera, diatoms), pollen, volcanic ash, and chemical indicators. The types of organisms, their isotopic composition, and sediment composition reflect past ocean temperatures, salinity, currents, productivity, and terrestrial climate.
      • **What they tell us:** Past ocean temperatures, sea level, ocean circulation, marine productivity, terrestrial vegetation, and glacial cycles.
      • **How far back:** Tens of millions to **hundreds of millions of years** (limited by the age of the ocean floor).
    • **Historical Data (Documentary Evidence):**
      • **How used:** Written records from human history, such as ship logs, harvest records, diaries, paintings, and government documents.
      • **What they tell us:** Past weather events (floods, droughts, severe winters), harvest yields, dates of river freezing/thawing, and general climate conditions.
      • **How far back:** A few hundred to **a few thousand years** (limited by the existence of written records).
    • **Coral Reefs:**
      • **How used:** Corals grow by adding annual layers, similar to tree rings. The chemical composition (e.g., oxygen isotopes, trace elements) within these layers reflects past ocean temperatures, salinity, and water chemistry.
      • **What they tell us:** Past sea surface temperatures, ocean acidity, salinity, and tropical storm activity.
      • **How far back:** Hundreds to **thousands of years** (limited by coral lifespan and preservation).
    • **Tree Cores (Dendroclimatology):**
      • **How used:** Cores are extracted from trees, revealing annual growth rings. The width, density, and isotopic composition of these rings are influenced by climate conditions during the year of growth.
      • **What they tell us:** Past temperature, precipitation (droughts/wet periods), and fire history.
      • **How far back:** Hundreds to **thousands of years** (limited by the lifespan of long-lived trees and availability of ancient wood).
    • **Pollens and Pack Rat Middens:**
      • **Pollens:** Preserved pollen grains (e.g., in lake sediments, ice cores, soil) indicate the types of plants that grew in an area in the past, which in turn reflects past climate and vegetation zones.
      • **Pack Rat Middens:** Accumulated debris (plant fragments, seeds, bones, droppings) collected by pack rats and preserved in arid caves. These provide a direct record of past local vegetation and climate in dry regions.
      • **What they tell us:** Past vegetation composition, which serves as a proxy for past temperature and precipitation.
      • **How far back:** Tens of thousands to **hundreds of thousands of years**.

Is the heating that we are experiencing today on a similar time scale to these past cycles, or much faster or slower?

The heating that we are experiencing today is occurring on a time scale that is **much, much faster** than past natural climate cycles (like those driven by Milankovitch cycles or other geological processes). While Earth's climate has changed naturally throughout history, the current rate of warming, particularly since the mid-20th century, is unprecedented in the geological record of the last tens of thousands to millions of years. This rapid rate is a key indicator that the current warming is not part of a natural cycle but is instead driven by human activities.

Climate Change Impacts

How is Earth being impacted by a warming climate? Make sure you can explain how a warming climate is causing changes to each of the following, and how these vary by region:

  • **Tropical Storms (Hurricanes/Typhoons/Cyclones):**
    • **Impact:** A warming climate is leading to **fewer but potentially more intense** tropical storms. Warmer ocean waters provide more energy for storms to strengthen, leading to higher wind speeds, heavier rainfall, and larger storm surges.
    • **Regional Variation:** Impacts are concentrated in tropical and subtropical coastal regions. Regions with warmer oceans (e.g., Atlantic, Pacific, Indian Ocean basins) will see these changes. Coastal communities face increased risk of damage and flooding.
  • **Heat Waves:**
    • **Impact:** Warming climate is causing **more frequent, longer-lasting, and more intense** heat waves.
    • **Regional Variation:** Affects many populated regions globally, especially urban areas (exacerbated by urban heat island effect) and regions with traditionally hot climates. Vulnerable populations (elderly, outdoor workers, those without AC) are at higher risk.
  • **Drought:**
    • **Impact:** A warming climate can lead to **increased frequency, severity, and duration of droughts** in many regions. Higher temperatures increase evaporation from land and water bodies, drying out soils and reducing water availability. Changes in atmospheric circulation can also shift precipitation patterns.
    • **Regional Variation:** Impacts are severe in already arid/semi-arid regions (e.g., Southwestern U.S., parts of Africa, Australia, Mediterranean), leading to water scarcity, agricultural losses, and increased wildfire risk.
  • **Flooding:**
    • **Impact:** A warming climate contributes to **increased flooding** through multiple mechanisms:
      • **More Intense Rainfall:** Warmer atmosphere holds more moisture, leading to heavier downpours.
      • **Sea Level Rise:** Exacerbates coastal flooding during high tides and storm surges.
      • **Rapid Snowmelt:** Warmer temperatures can lead to faster spring snowmelt, overwhelming river systems.
    • **Regional Variation:** Impacts are diverse: coastal areas (sea level rise), river basins (heavy rainfall, snowmelt), and urban areas (impervious surfaces, overwhelmed drainage).
  • **Ice Sheets and Glaciers:**
    • **Impact:** Global warming is causing **accelerated melting and retreat** of ice sheets (Greenland, Antarctica) and mountain glaciers worldwide. This contributes significantly to sea level rise.
    • **Regional Variation:** Most pronounced in the Arctic (Greenland, Arctic sea ice) and Antarctic regions, but also affects mountain ranges globally (e.g., Himalayas, Andes, Rockies, Alps), impacting water supplies for downstream communities.
  • **Sea Level Change (2 major factors/causes):**
    • **Impact:** Global mean sea level is rising at an accelerating rate.
    • **Major Factors/Causes:**
      1. **Thermal Expansion:** As ocean water warms, it expands, taking up more space. This is a significant contributor to sea level rise.
      2. **Melting of Glaciers and Ice Sheets:** Water from melting land-based ice (mountain glaciers, Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets) flows into the oceans, adding volume.
    • **Regional Variation:** Coastal communities worldwide are impacted, with low-lying islands and delta regions being particularly vulnerable to inundation, increased storm surge, and saltwater intrusion.
  • **Animal Habitats:**
    • **Impact:** Warming climate is causing **habitat shifts, degradation, and loss**. Species adapted to specific temperature ranges or conditions find their habitats shrinking or moving.
    • **Regional Variation:** Affects all biomes. Polar habitats (e.g., sea ice for polar bears) are shrinking rapidly. Coral reefs are experiencing bleaching due to ocean warming/acidification. Forests face increased wildfire risk.
  • **Animal Migration:**
    • **Impact:** Many animal species are **shifting their geographical ranges and migration patterns** in response to changing temperatures, altered growing seasons, and modified food availability. Species are moving towards poles or higher altitudes.
    • **Regional Variation:** Observable globally. Birds are migrating earlier, fish are moving to cooler waters, and insects are expanding their ranges. This can disrupt ecosystems and agricultural systems.
  • **Increase in the Spread of Disease:**
    • **Impact:** A warming climate can lead to an **increase in the spread of vector-borne diseases** (e.g., malaria, dengue fever, Zika virus) as warmer temperatures expand the geographical range of disease-carrying insects (vectors) like mosquitoes and ticks. It can also impact waterborne diseases due to altered precipitation and flooding.
    • **Regional Variation:** Impacts are most pronounced in tropical and subtropical regions where vectors thrive, but also in temperate regions where vectors are expanding their range.
  • **Ocean Acidification:**
    • **Impact:** The ocean absorbs a significant portion of the excess $CO_2$ from the atmosphere. When $CO_2$ dissolves in seawater, it forms carbonic acid, increasing the ocean's acidity (lowering pH). This is distinct from ocean warming, though both are caused by $CO_2$ emissions.
    • **Regional Variation:** Affects all oceans. Polar waters and upwelling regions can be particularly vulnerable due to naturally lower pH or colder temperatures that allow more $CO_2$ absorption.
    • **Consequences:** Harms marine organisms that build shells or skeletons from calcium carbonate (e.g., corals, shellfish, plankton), making it harder for them to form and maintain their structures. This impacts marine food webs and ecosystems.
  • **Ocean Warming:**
    • **Impact:** Oceans absorb over 90% of the excess heat trapped by greenhouse gases, leading to a steady increase in ocean temperatures.
    • **Regional Variation:** Affects all oceans, but particularly pronounced in surface waters.
    • **Consequences:**
      • **Coral Bleaching:** Warmer water stresses corals, causing them to expel algae and turn white, often leading to death.
      • **Species Migration:** Marine species move to cooler waters, disrupting ecosystems and fisheries.
      • **Sea Level Rise:** Contributes to thermal expansion.
      • **Reduced Oxygen:** Warmer water holds less dissolved oxygen.
      • **More Intense Storms:** Provides more energy for tropical cyclones.

What is the difference between mitigation and adaptation when it comes to climate change? What are some strategies for each?

  • **Mitigation:**
    • **Definition:** Actions taken to **reduce or prevent the emission of greenhouse gases** into the atmosphere, or to enhance the removal of existing greenhouse gases from the atmosphere, thereby **reducing the severity of future climate change**. It aims to address the root causes of climate change.
    • **Strategies:**
      • **Transition to Renewable Energy:** Shifting from fossil fuels to solar, wind, hydro, geothermal power.
      • **Energy Efficiency & Conservation:** Reducing energy consumption in homes, transport, industry (e.g., insulation, LED lighting, efficient vehicles).
      • **Carbon Capture & Storage (CCS):** Technologies that capture $CO_2$ from industrial sources and store it underground.
      • **Afforestation & Reforestation:** Planting new trees or restoring forests to absorb $CO_2$.
      • **Sustainable Agriculture & Land Management:** Practices that reduce emissions and enhance carbon sequestration in soils.
      • **Waste Reduction & Improved Waste Management:** Reducing methane emissions from landfills.
  • **Adaptation:**
    • **Definition:** Actions taken to **adjust to the actual or expected impacts of climate change**, thereby **reducing vulnerability and building resilience** to its effects. It acknowledges that some level of climate change is already unavoidable and focuses on coping with the consequences.
    • **Strategies:**
      • **Building Sea Walls & Levees:** Protecting coastal communities from sea level rise and storm surges.
      • **Developing Drought-Resistant Crops:** Adapting agriculture to changing water availability.
      • **Improving Early Warning Systems:** For extreme weather events (e.g., heat waves, floods).
      • **Relocating Infrastructure:** Moving buildings and communities away from vulnerable coastal areas or floodplains.
      • **Developing Heat-Resilient Cities:** Increasing green spaces, using reflective surfaces, establishing cooling centers.
      • **Water Conservation & Diversification:** Implementing water-saving measures and exploring alternative water sources.
      • **Ecosystem Restoration:** Restoring wetlands and mangroves to act as natural buffers against storms and floods.
