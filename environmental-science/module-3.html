Atmosphere

What three chemical (element) building blocks for life come from the atmosphere?

The three chemical (element) building blocks for life that come from the atmosphere are:

  • Carbon (C): From carbon dioxide ($CO_2$)
  • Nitrogen (N): From nitrogen gas ($N_2$)
  • Oxygen (O): From oxygen gas ($O_2$) and carbon dioxide ($CO_2$)

How thick is the atmosphere? Where are most gases located? How does gas density change with altitude, or distance from Earth’s surface?

  • The atmosphere does not have a distinct upper boundary, but it gradually thins out into space. Roughly 99% of the total mass of the atmosphere is located within about 32 kilometers (20 miles) of Earth's surface.
  • Most gases are located closest to Earth's surface due to gravity.
  • Gas density decreases with increasing altitude (distance from Earth’s surface). This is because the weight of the air above compresses the air below, making it denser at lower altitudes.

What are the four main gases in Earth’s atmosphere, and how much water vapor is present?

The four main gases in Earth's atmosphere are:

  1. Nitrogen ($N_2$): Approximately 78%
  2. Oxygen ($O_2$): Approximately 21%
  3. Argon (Ar): Approximately 0.93%
  4. Carbon Dioxide ($CO_2$): Approximately 0.04%

Water vapor is a variable gas, meaning its concentration changes significantly. It can range from nearly 0% in very cold, dry regions to up to 4% in warm, humid regions. On average, it constitutes about 0.25% of the atmosphere's mass.

List and describe the four layers of Earth’s atmosphere.

The four main layers of Earth's atmosphere, from lowest to highest, are:

  1. Troposphere:
    • Description: This is the lowest layer, extending from the surface up to about 8-15 km (5-9 miles). It's where all weather occurs.
    • Climate Patterns: Temperature generally decreases with altitude. Contains most of the atmosphere's mass and water vapor.
  2. Stratosphere:
    • Description: Extends from the top of the troposphere to about 50 km (31 miles). Contains the ozone layer.
    • Climate Patterns: Temperature generally increases with altitude due to the absorption of ultraviolet (UV) radiation by the ozone layer.
  3. Mesosphere:
    • Description: Extends from the top of the stratosphere to about 85 km (53 miles).
    • Climate Patterns: Temperature generally decreases with altitude, reaching the coldest temperatures in the atmosphere (around $-90^\circ C$ or $-130^\circ F$). Most meteors burn up in this layer.
  4. Thermosphere:
    • Description: Extends from the top of the mesosphere to about 600 km (370 miles) or more, gradually blending into space.
    • Climate Patterns: Temperature increases significantly with altitude due to the absorption of high-energy solar radiation by sparse gas molecules, but the air is so thin that it would feel very cold. The International Space Station orbits in this layer. The ionosphere, a region of ionized gases, is found within the thermosphere.

Define air pressure.

Air pressure is the force exerted by the weight of the air molecules above a given surface. It is essentially the force per unit area exerted by the column of air above that point.

How does temperature and air pressure change with altitude, or distance from Earth’s surface?

  • Temperature:
    • In the troposphere, temperature generally decreases with increasing altitude.
    • In the stratosphere, temperature generally increases with increasing altitude.
    • In the mesosphere, temperature generally decreases with increasing altitude.
    • In the thermosphere, temperature generally increases with increasing altitude.
  • Air Pressure: Air pressure consistently decreases with increasing altitude (distance from Earth’s surface). This is because there are fewer air molecules above you to exert pressure as you go higher.

Weather & Climate

What are similarities and differences between weather and climate? What variables describe them?

Similarities:

  • Both describe atmospheric conditions.
  • Both are influenced by similar atmospheric variables.

Differences:

  • Weather: Refers to the short-term, day-to-day atmospheric conditions at a specific place and time. It's what you experience outside right now or in the next few days.
  • Climate: Refers to the long-term average of weather patterns in a region, typically over 30 years or more. It describes the typical weather for a given season or location.

Variables that describe them:

  • Temperature
  • Precipitation (rain, snow, hail, etc.)
  • Humidity
  • Air Pressure
  • Wind Speed and Direction
  • Cloud Cover
  • Sunshine

Global Convection Currents

What causes differential heating on Earth? How does this influence weather patterns?

Differential heating on Earth is primarily caused by:

  1. Angle of Solar Radiation: The sun's rays strike the Earth more directly (at a higher angle) near the equator, spreading over a smaller area and leading to more intense heating. Near the poles, the rays strike at a lower angle, spreading over a larger area and leading to less intense heating.
  2. Albedo: Different surfaces reflect different amounts of solar radiation. Ice and snow have high albedo (reflect a lot), while dark surfaces like oceans and forests have low albedo (absorb a lot).
  3. Presence of Water: Water has a high specific heat capacity, meaning it heats up and cools down more slowly than land. This moderates temperatures in coastal areas.
  4. Cloud Cover: Clouds can reflect incoming solar radiation, reducing heating.

This differential heating creates temperature and pressure gradients, which in turn drive atmospheric and oceanic circulation, influencing global weather patterns, including wind systems, ocean currents, and precipitation zones.

What parts of the globe receive the most rainfall, and which parts receive the least?

  • Most Rainfall: Regions near the equator (around $0^\circ$ latitude) and along the mid-latitudes ($40^\circ - 60^\circ$ latitude) tend to receive the most rainfall. This includes tropical rainforests, parts of Southeast Asia, and temperate regions with frequent storm systems.
  • Least Rainfall: Regions around $30^\circ$ North and South latitudes (where most of the world's major deserts are located) and the polar regions receive the least rainfall.

Where is Earth heating more and where is heat escaping?

  • Heating More: Earth is heating more in the tropical and equatorial regions where solar radiation is most direct and intense.
  • Heat Escaping: Heat is escaping (radiating back into space) more in the polar regions where solar radiation is less intense and surfaces are often reflective. The atmosphere and oceans act to redistribute this heat from the equator towards the poles.

Describe the flow of air and moisture in the three global convection currents (Hadley cell, Ferrel cell, and Polar cell) and how this influences rainfall patterns.

Earth's global atmospheric circulation is driven by three major convection cells in each hemisphere:

  1. Hadley Cell ($0^\circ - 30^\circ$ latitude):
    • Flow: Warm, moist air at the equator ($0^\circ$) rises, creating a zone of low pressure (the Intertropical Convergence Zone - ITCZ). As it rises, it cools, condenses, and releases moisture as heavy rainfall. The now dry, cool air then moves poleward at high altitudes, descends around $30^\circ$ latitude (creating high-pressure zones), and flows back towards the equator as trade winds near the surface.
    • Rainfall Patterns: Causes abundant rainfall near the equator (tropical rainforests) and dry conditions/deserts around $30^\circ$ North and South.
  2. Ferrel Cell ($30^\circ - 60^\circ$ latitude):
    • Flow: This is a less distinct and more complex cell, driven indirectly by the Hadley and Polar cells. Air at $30^\circ$ latitude descends (from the Hadley cell) and flows poleward along the surface. This air meets cold air flowing from the poles around $60^\circ$ latitude, causing the warmer air to rise (forming the polar front) and move equatorward at high altitudes before descending again at $30^\circ$.
    • Rainfall Patterns: Causes moderate rainfall and variable weather in the mid-latitudes ($40^\circ - 60^\circ$) due to the rising air at the polar front, leading to temperate forests and grasslands.
  3. Polar Cell ($60^\circ - 90^\circ$ latitude):
    • Flow: Cold, dense air at the poles ($90^\circ$) sinks, creating high pressure. This cold air flows equatorward along the surface, meeting warmer air from the Ferrel cell around $60^\circ$ latitude. The warmer air rises (forming the polar front), moves poleward at high altitudes, and then descends back at the poles.
    • Rainfall Patterns: Causes very dry conditions at the poles (polar deserts) due to the sinking cold air, and some precipitation around $60^\circ$ latitude where air is rising.

What is the Coriolis effect, and what causes it?

The Coriolis effect is an apparent force that deflects moving objects (like air currents, ocean currents, and projectiles) from a straight path on a rotating body like Earth.

  • Cause: It is caused by Earth's rotation. As Earth spins, points at the equator are moving faster than points closer to the poles. An object moving from the equator towards the pole carries its initial eastward velocity, causing it to appear to deflect eastward relative to the slower-moving ground beneath it. Conversely, an object moving from the pole towards the equator appears to deflect westward.
  • Direction of Deflection:
    • In the Northern Hemisphere, it deflects moving objects to the right.
    • In the Southern Hemisphere, it deflects moving objects to the left.

What is the jetstream, and what causes it? Which direction does it travel across Earth? How does this impact air travel?

  • What it is: The jet stream is a narrow, fast-flowing, meandering air current found in the upper levels of the atmosphere (troposphere and lower stratosphere). They are typically thousands of kilometers long, a few hundred kilometers wide, and a few kilometers thick.
  • Causes: Jet streams are caused by the large temperature differences between air masses (e.g., cold polar air and warm tropical air) and the Coriolis effect. The greater the temperature difference, the stronger the pressure gradient, and thus the faster the wind.
  • Direction of Travel: Jet streams generally travel from west to east across Earth, following the global circulation patterns. There are typically two main jet streams in each hemisphere: the polar jet stream (stronger, closer to the poles) and the subtropical jet stream (weaker, closer to the equator).
  • Impact on Air Travel:
    • Eastbound flights: Aircraft flying eastward can utilize the tailwinds of the jet stream, significantly reducing flight time and fuel consumption.
    • Westbound flights: Aircraft flying westward often try to avoid the jet stream's headwinds, which can increase flight time and fuel consumption. Pilots may choose to fly at lower altitudes or take different routes to minimize this effect.

Rain Shadow Effect

What is the rain shadow effect?

The rain shadow effect is a meteorological phenomenon that creates a dry region, often a desert, on the leeward (downwind) side of a mountain range.

What type of air is on the leeward and windward side of a mountain that causes a rain shadow?

  • Windward Side: On the windward side (the side facing the prevailing wind), the air is typically moist and cool. As moist air is forced to rise over the mountain, it cools, expands, and its water vapor condenses, leading to clouds and precipitation.
  • Leeward Side: On the leeward side (the side sheltered from the wind), the air is typically dry and warm. After releasing its moisture on the windward side, the now dry air descends the leeward side. As it descends, it compresses and warms, leading to clear skies and arid conditions.

What types of features are caused by rain shadows, and where are some of the big ones located?

  • Features: Rain shadows cause arid and semi-arid features such as deserts, dry grasslands, and steppes.
  • Big Ones Located:
    • Mojave Desert, Great Basin Desert, and Sonoran Desert in the western United States (east of the Sierra Nevada mountains).
    • Atacama Desert in Chile (east of the Andes mountains).
    • Patagonian Desert in Argentina (east of the Andes mountains).
    • Gobi Desert in Mongolia and China (south of the Altai Mountains and north of the Himalayas).
    • Parts of the Tibetan Plateau (north of the Himalayas).

Monsoons

What is a monsoon?

A monsoon is a seasonal reversal of wind patterns, characterized by a distinct wet season and a distinct dry season. They are caused by the differential heating of land and sea.

Where is the world’s largest monsoon?

The world's largest and most well-known monsoon system is the Indian Monsoon, which affects South Asia and Southeast Asia.

How many people are impacted by monsoonal climates?

Globally, billions of people are impacted by monsoonal climates, particularly in Asia, Africa, and parts of North and South America. Estimates often place the number at over half of the world's population.

What time of year is it dry or wet in monsoonal regions? What is the seasonal pattern for monsoons in the northern hemisphere (such as in India and Arizona - southwestern U.S.) and what drives this pattern?

  • Wet Season: Typically occurs during the summer months (e.g., June to September in the Northern Hemisphere).
  • Dry Season: Typically occurs during the winter months (e.g., October to May in the Northern Hemisphere).

Seasonal Pattern for Monsoons in the Northern Hemisphere (e.g., India, Arizona):

  • Summer (Wet Season):
    • Pattern: During summer, land heats up much faster and to a higher temperature than the ocean. This creates a strong low-pressure system over the land. The relatively cooler, higher-pressure air over the ocean (which is also humid) is drawn inland towards the low-pressure system. This inflow of moist ocean air leads to widespread heavy rainfall.
    • Driving Force: Differential heating between land and ocean.
  • Winter (Dry Season):
    • Pattern: During winter, the land cools down much faster and to a lower temperature than the ocean. This creates a strong high-pressure system over the land. The relatively warmer, lower-pressure air over the ocean draws the dry, cool air from the land outwards towards the ocean. This outflow of dry air leads to very little or no precipitation.
    • Driving Force: Differential cooling between land and ocean.

Climate Zones

What are the Koppen climate zones? Make sure you can match each zone with a basic description of that zone (where it’s generally found, its climate patterns).

The Köppen climate classification system is one of the most widely used climate classification systems. It divides climates into five main groups (A, B, C, D, E) based on temperature and precipitation patterns, which are then further subdivided.

Here are the five main Köppen climate zones with basic descriptions:

  1. A - Tropical Climates:
    • General Location: Near the equator ($0^\circ$ latitude), high rainfall, high temperatures (e.g., Amazon Basin, Congo Basin, Southeast Asia).
    • Climate Patterns: High temperatures year-round (average monthly temperature above $18^\circ C$ or $64.4^\circ F$). High humidity and significant precipitation.
    • Subtypes: Tropical Rainforest (Af), Tropical Monsoon (Am), Tropical Savanna (Aw).
  2. B - Dry (Arid and Semi-Arid) Climates:
    • General Location: Often found around $20^\circ - 35^\circ$ North and South latitudes (subtropical high-pressure belts) and in rain shadows.
    • Climate Patterns: Low precipitation, high evaporation rates. Can have large diurnal (daily) temperature ranges.
    • Subtypes: Desert (BW), Steppe (BS).
  3. C - Temperate (Mesothermal) Climates:
    • General Location: Mid-latitudes, typically between $30^\circ - 50^\circ$ North and South latitudes.
    • Climate Patterns: Distinct seasons with warm/hot summers and mild winters. Moderate precipitation.
    • Subtypes: Mediterranean (Cs), Humid Subtropical (Cfa), Marine West Coast (Cfb).
  4. D - Continental (Microthermal) Climates:
    • General Location: Interior of continents in the mid- to high-latitudes, typically $40^\circ - 70^\circ$ North latitude (primarily in the Northern Hemisphere due to large landmasses).
    • Climate Patterns: Significant seasonal temperature variations with cold winters (at least one month below $0^\circ C$ or $32^\circ F$) and warm/hot summers. Moderate precipitation, often concentrated in summer.
    • Subtypes: Humid Continental (Dfa, Dfb), Subarctic (Dfc, Dfd).
  5. E - Polar Climates:
    • General Location: High latitudes, near the poles (above $60^\circ$ North and South latitudes).
    • Climate Patterns: Extremely cold temperatures year-round (average monthly temperature below $10^\circ C$ or $50^\circ F$). Very low precipitation.
    • Subtypes: Tundra (ET), Ice Cap (EF).

Hydrosphere

What is the relative amount (%) of water in the different water reservoirs on Earth (oceans, glaciers and ice sheets, groundwater, surface water, atmosphere, biological water)? What is the relative distribution of FRESH water on Earth?

Relative Amount (%) of Water in Earth's Reservoirs (Approximate):

  • Oceans: ~96.5%
  • Glaciers and Ice Sheets: ~1.74%
  • Groundwater: ~1.7%
  • Surface Water (lakes, rivers, swamps): ~0.01%
  • Atmosphere: ~0.001%
  • Biological Water: ~0.0001%

Relative Distribution of FRESH Water on Earth (Approximate):

  • Glaciers and Ice Sheets: ~68.7%
  • Groundwater: ~30.1%
  • Surface Water (lakes, rivers, swamps): ~0.3%
  • Other (soil moisture, atmosphere, biological water): ~0.9%

How much of Earth’s water is fresh water, and how much is available for consumption/use (i.e., not salty, in ice sheets or polluted)?

  • Total Fresh Water: Approximately 2.5% of Earth's total water is fresh water.
  • Available for Consumption/Use: A very small fraction, less than 1% (some sources say less than 0.5%), is readily available for human consumption and use. This excludes saltwater, water locked in glaciers and ice sheets, and deep or polluted groundwater.

Define water cycle transfer processes such as evaporation (& humidity vs relative humidity), transpiration vs. evapotranspiration, condensation, precipitation, infiltration, runoff and recharge.

  • Evaporation: The process where liquid water turns into water vapor and rises into the atmosphere (from oceans, lakes, rivers, etc.).
  • Condensation: Water vapor in the atmosphere cools and changes back into liquid water droplets or ice crystals, forming clouds.
  • Precipitation: Water (in the form of rain, snow, sleet, hail) falls from clouds back to Earth's surface due to gravity.
  • Surface Runoff:** Water flows over the land surface, typically into streams, rivers, lakes, or directly into oceans, when the ground is saturated or precipitation is intense.
  • **Streamflow:** The movement of water within a river or stream channel.
  • **Infiltration:** Water seeps from the land surface down into the soil and rock layers below, becoming soil moisture or groundwater.
  • **Transpiration:** Water vapor released into the atmosphere from plants through their leaves.
  • **Evapotranspiration:** The combined process of evaporation from surfaces and transpiration from plants.

Order the water reservoirs on Earth with regard to the amount of time a drop of water resides in them (i.e., residence time).

From longest residence time to shortest:

  1. Glaciers and Ice Sheets: Thousands to hundreds of thousands of years.
  2. Deep Groundwater: Hundreds to thousands of years (can be millions for very deep aquifers).
  3. Oceans: Thousands of years (average around 3,000 years).
  4. Lakes: Days to hundreds of years (depends on size and flow).
  5. Rivers: Days to weeks.
  6. Atmosphere: Hours to days (average around 9 days).
  7. Biological Water: Hours to days.

Oceans

How big is the ocean and how many oceans are there on Earth? How much of Earth’s surface does it cover?

  • Size: The ocean is vast, containing about 1.35 billion cubic kilometers of water.
  • Number of Oceans: Traditionally, there are considered to be five named oceans: the Pacific, Atlantic, Indian, Southern (Antarctic), and Arctic Oceans. Some classifications still refer to four (excluding the Southern Ocean as a distinct entity).
  • Surface Coverage: The ocean covers approximately 71% of Earth's surface.

How many basins is the ocean (generally) divided into, and what are they?

The ocean is generally divided into four major basins:

  1. Pacific Ocean Basin: The largest and deepest.
  2. Atlantic Ocean Basin: The second largest.
  3. Indian Ocean Basin: The third largest.
  4. Arctic Ocean Basin: The smallest and shallowest. (The Southern Ocean is often considered an extension of these basins rather than a distinct basin in this context).

How much of Earth’s biosphere exists in the oceans?

The ocean contains the vast majority of Earth's biosphere. While estimates vary, it is often stated that over 90% of the Earth's habitable space (volume) is in the ocean, and it contains a significant portion of Earth's biodiversity, though much of it is still undiscovered.

The ocean is so vast - how have humans impacted the ocean?

Despite its vastness, humans have significantly impacted the ocean in numerous ways:

  • **Pollution:** Plastic pollution, chemical runoff (from agriculture and industry), oil spills, and wastewater.
  • **Overfishing:** Depletion of fish stocks, disruption of marine food webs, and bycatch.
  • **Habitat Destruction:** Damage to coral reefs, mangrove forests, and seafloor habitats from destructive fishing practices (e.g., bottom trawling), coastal development, and pollution.
  • **Climate Change:**
    • **Ocean Acidification:** Absorption of excess carbon dioxide from the atmosphere makes the ocean more acidic, impacting shell-forming organisms.
    • **Ocean Warming:** Leading to coral bleaching, species migration, and changes in ocean currents.
    • **Sea Level Rise:** From thermal expansion of water and melting glaciers/ice sheets.
  • **Noise Pollution:** From shipping, seismic surveys, and military sonar, impacting marine mammal communication and navigation.
  • **Invasive Species:** Introduction of non-native species through ballast water and shipping.

Glaciers and Ice Sheets

How much ice is in Greenland and Antarctica? If all of the ice in these two vast ice sheets melted, how much sea level rise would Earth experience?

  • Amount of Ice:
    • Greenland Ice Sheet: Holds enough ice to raise global sea levels by approximately 7.4 meters (24 feet).
    • Antarctic Ice Sheet: Holds enough ice to raise global sea levels by approximately 58 meters (190 feet).
  • Total Sea Level Rise if both melted: If all the ice in Greenland and Antarctica melted, global sea levels would rise by about 65-66 meters (214-217 feet).

What is a “healthy” glacier? What does it mean when glaciers are “in balance”? What is the source of ice in a glacier?

  • "Healthy" Glacier: A "healthy" glacier is one that is maintaining its size or growing, meaning its accumulation of snow and ice is greater than or equal to its ablation (melting and sublimation).
  • Glaciers "in balance": A glacier is "in balance" when the rate of ice accumulation (from snowfall) in its upper reaches equals the rate of ice loss (from melting, sublimation, and calving) in its lower reaches. This means its mass balance is zero, and its size and position remain relatively stable over time.
  • Source of Ice: The source of ice in a glacier is accumulated snowfall that compacts and transforms into ice over time.

Do glaciers flow? How do they move and flow? Hint: what happens at the bottom of a glacier?

Yes, glaciers flow. They are not static blocks of ice.

  • How they move and flow: Glaciers move through two primary mechanisms:
    1. Internal Deformation (Creep): Under the immense pressure of its own weight, ice behaves as a plastic material and deforms internally. Ice crystals slide past each other, allowing the glacier to flow slowly downhill. This is more dominant in colder, polar glaciers.
    2. Basal Sliding: This occurs when the base of the glacier slides over the bedrock beneath it. This happens when meltwater accumulates at the bottom of the glacier, acting as a lubricant. This process is very important for faster-moving glaciers, especially temperate glaciers.
  • What happens at the bottom: At the bottom of a glacier, especially if it's temperate, meltwater can form due to pressure melting or geothermal heat. This meltwater reduces friction between the ice and the bedrock, allowing the glacier to slide more easily (basal sliding).

What is firn? How long does it take for snow to turn into glacial ice?

  • Firn: Firn is partially compacted granular snow that is an intermediate stage between fresh snow and glacial ice. It has a density greater than fresh snow but less than glacial ice. It forms as snow crystals undergo compaction, melting, and refreezing.
  • Time to turn into glacial ice: The time it takes for snow to turn into glacial ice varies significantly depending on climate and snowfall rates. It can range from:
    • A few years (e.g., 5-10 years) in areas with high snowfall and temperate conditions.
    • Hundreds to thousands of years in very cold, dry polar regions where accumulation rates are low.

How do crevasses form in a glacier?

Crevasses are deep, vertical cracks or fissures that form in the brittle upper layer of a glacier. They form when the glacier flows over irregular terrain, changes in slope, or when different parts of the glacier move at different speeds, causing the ice to stretch or pull apart faster than it can deform plastically. The brittle ice fractures under the stress.

How do glaciers recede? What is the process?

Glaciers recede (or retreat) when the rate of ice loss (ablation) exceeds the rate of ice accumulation. It's important to note that the glacier itself is still flowing forward, but its front (terminus) is melting back faster than the ice is advancing.

The process involves:

  1. Increased Melting: Higher temperatures lead to more surface melting.
  2. Increased Sublimation: Ice turning directly into water vapor.
  3. Increased Calving: For tidewater glaciers, warmer ocean waters can cause more rapid breaking off of icebergs at the terminus.
  4. Reduced Snowfall: Less snow accumulation means less new ice to replenish the glacier.

When ablation outpaces accumulation, the glacier's mass balance becomes negative, and its terminus appears to move uphill or shrink.

Where is most of the water stored in ice sheets (which 2 locations) on Earth?

Most of the water stored in ice sheets on Earth is located in the Antarctic Ice Sheet and the Greenland Ice Sheet.

Surface & Groundwater Resources

What is a watershed and what happens to water that falls on a watershed as precipitation? How big is a watershed?

  • Watershed (or Drainage Basin): A watershed is an area of land where all of the water that falls as precipitation (rain, snow) drains off and eventually flows to a common outlet, such as a river, lake, reservoir, bay, or ocean. It's defined by topographic divides (ridges, hills).
  • What happens to water: When precipitation falls on a watershed, it can:
    1. Evaporate/Transpire: Return to the atmosphere.
    2. Infiltrate: Soak into the ground to become soil moisture or groundwater.
    3. Runoff: Flow over the land surface as surface runoff, eventually entering streams and rivers within that watershed.
  • How big is a watershed: Watersheds can vary enormously in size, from a few square kilometers (e.g., a small stream's drainage area) to millions of square kilometers (e.g., the Amazon River basin or the Mississippi River basin). Larger watersheds are often composed of many smaller sub-watersheds.

What are some examples of freshwater biome water bodies? What is a stream/river, and what are the three largest source of water that flows into rivers? What is a lake, and how does it differ from a pond?

  • Examples of Freshwater Biome Water Bodies: Rivers, streams, lakes, ponds, wetlands (swamps, marshes, bogs), and groundwater.
  • Stream/River: A stream is a small, flowing body of fresh water, while a river is a larger, natural flowing watercourse, usually freshwater, flowing towards an ocean, sea, lake, or another river.
  • Three largest sources of water that flows into rivers:
    1. Surface Runoff: Water flowing over the land surface after precipitation.
    2. Groundwater Discharge (Baseflow): Water that infiltrates the ground and then flows laterally to emerge into the river channel.
    3. Direct Precipitation: Rain or snow falling directly into the river channel.
  • Lake vs. Pond:
    • Lake: A large, usually permanent, body of standing fresh water, typically deeper and larger than a pond. Lakes often have distinct thermal stratification and a larger open-water zone.
    • Pond: A smaller, shallower body of standing fresh water. Ponds are typically shallow enough for sunlight to reach the bottom, allowing aquatic plants to grow across the entire basin. They usually do not exhibit distinct thermal stratification. The distinction can sometimes be subjective.

What are wetlands? What is the difference between a swamp and a marsh?

  • Wetlands: Wetlands are areas where water covers the soil, or is present either at or near the surface of the soil all year or for varying periods of time during the year, including during the growing season. They are characterized by unique soil (hydric soils) and vegetation (hydrophytes) adapted to waterlogged conditions.
  • Swamp vs. Marsh:
    • Swamp: A wetland dominated by woody plants (trees and shrubs). Swamps are typically forested wetlands.
    • Marsh: A wetland dominated by herbaceous (non-woody) plants like grasses, reeds, and sedges. Marshes are typically characterized by emergent vegetation.

What IS groundwater? Where do you find it? What do you call a body of groundwater?

  • Groundwater: Groundwater is water that has infiltrated the ground and is stored in the pore spaces of soil, sediment, and rock formations beneath the Earth's surface.
  • Where you find it: You find groundwater in the saturated zone below the water table. It fills the cracks, fissures, and pores in geological formations.
  • Body of Groundwater: A body of groundwater is called an **aquifer**.

Define water table, saturated zone and unsaturated zone.

  • Water Table: The **water table** is the upper surface of the saturated zone, where the pore spaces in the ground are completely filled with water. It rises and falls with changes in precipitation and groundwater pumping.
  • Saturated Zone: The **saturated zone** (or phreatic zone) is the area beneath the water table where all the pore spaces in the rock and soil are completely filled with water.
  • Unsaturated Zone: The **unsaturated zone** (or vadose zone) is the area above the water table where the pore spaces in the rock and soil are not completely filled with water; they contain both air and water. Water in this zone is typically moving downward towards the water table.

Differentiate between gaining and losing stream, and the characteristics of each. What is a spring?

  • Gaining Stream (Effluent Stream):
    • Characteristics: A stream that gains water from groundwater through its bed. The water table is higher than the stream bed, causing groundwater to flow into the stream.
    • Appearance: Often maintains flow even during dry periods, as it is fed by groundwater.
  • Losing Stream (Influent Stream):
    • Characteristics: A stream that loses water to the groundwater system through its bed. The water table is lower than the stream bed, causing stream water to seep down into the ground.
    • Appearance: May dry up during dry periods, as its water infiltrates the ground. Can contribute to aquifer recharge.
  • Spring: A spring is a natural outflow of groundwater from the Earth's surface where the water table intersects the land surface, or where groundwater emerges from a crack or fissure in the rock.

How long is water in an aquifer (range of time)?

The residence time of water in an aquifer can vary greatly, ranging from days to thousands of years, and in some very deep or confined aquifers, it can be millions of years. It depends on factors like the aquifer's permeability, size, depth, and the rate of recharge and discharge.

How do we use groundwater? What happens if we pump water out of the ground faster than it is replenished?

  • Uses of Groundwater:
    • Drinking Water: A primary source for municipal and private wells.
    • Agriculture: Irrigation for crops.
    • Industry: Cooling, processing, and other industrial uses.
    • Livestock: Water for animals.
  • Consequences of Over-pumping (Pumping faster than replenished):
    • Lowering of the Water Table: The water table drops, requiring deeper wells and increasing pumping costs.
    • Well Depletion: Existing wells may go dry.
    • Land Subsidence: As water is removed from pore spaces, the overlying land can compact and sink, leading to irreversible ground-level lowering and damage to infrastructure.
    • Saltwater Intrusion: In coastal areas, over-pumping can draw saltwater from the ocean into freshwater aquifers, making the water unusable.
    • Reduced Streamflow/Springflow: Aquifers that feed streams and springs may no longer be able to sustain them, leading to reduced flow or drying up.
    • Increased Pumping Costs: Deeper pumping requires more energy and thus higher costs.
    • Groundwater Contamination: Changes in flow patterns can draw contaminants into wells.

Lithosphere

Rocks to Soil: Weathering, Erosion & Deposition

Define the three main categories of rocks, and describe how each is formed.

  • Igneous Rocks: Formed from the cooling and solidification of molten rock (magma or lava).
    • **Intrusive (Plutonic):** Form when magma cools slowly beneath Earth's surface, resulting in large crystals (e.g., granite).
    • **Extrusive (Volcanic):** Form when lava cools quickly on or above Earth's surface, resulting in small or no crystals (e.g., basalt, obsidian).
  • Sedimentary Rocks: Formed from the accumulation and compaction of sediments (fragments of other rocks, minerals, or organic matter) or by chemical precipitation.
    • **Clastic:** Formed from weathered and eroded rock fragments (e.g., sandstone, shale).
    • **Chemical:** Formed from precipitation of minerals from water (e.g., limestone, rock salt).
    • **Organic:** Formed from the accumulation of organic material (e.g., coal, some limestones).
  • Metamorphic Rocks: Formed when existing rocks (igneous, sedimentary, or other metamorphic rocks) are subjected to intense heat, pressure, or chemical alteration, causing them to change in mineral composition, texture, or structure without melting.
    • **Foliated:** Have a layered or banded appearance due to pressure (e.g., slate, schist, gneiss).
    • **Non-foliated:** Do not have a layered appearance (e.g., marble, quartzite).

Describe the processes of weathering, erosion, and deposition (differentiate between each process).

  • Weathering: The process of **breaking down** rocks, soil, and minerals, as well as wood and artificial materials, through contact with the Earth's atmosphere, biota, and waters. It does not involve the removal of rock material.
    • **Physical (Mechanical) Weathering:** Breaking rock into smaller pieces without changing its chemical composition (e.g., frost wedging, abrasion).
    • **Chemical Weathering:** Breaking down rock through chemical reactions, altering its composition (e.g., dissolution, oxidation).
  • Erosion: The process of **transporting** weathered rock and soil material from one location to another. It involves the movement of solids (sediment, soil, rock and other particles) by wind, water, ice, or gravity.
  • Deposition: The process by which eroded material is **laid down or settled** in a new location. This occurs when the transporting agent (wind, water, ice) loses energy and can no longer carry the sediment.

Describe the different forms of physical weathering and explain how chemical weathering happens. What types of landforms often result from chemical weathering?

Forms of Physical Weathering:

  • **Frost Wedging (Freeze-Thaw):** Water seeps into cracks in rocks, freezes and expands (by ~9%), putting pressure on the rock and eventually breaking it apart.
  • **Abrasion:** The grinding and wearing away of rock surfaces by the friction and impact of rock particles carried by wind, water, or ice.
  • **Exfoliation (Pressure Release):** Overlying rock is removed by erosion, reducing pressure on the underlying rock. This causes the rock to expand and fracture in concentric layers, like an onion.
  • **Thermal Expansion/Contraction:** Repeated heating and cooling (e.g., day/night cycles in deserts) causes minerals in rocks to expand and contract at different rates, leading to fracturing.
  • **Salt Crystal Growth:** In arid regions, saline water evaporates from rock pores, leaving behind salt crystals. As these crystals grow, they exert pressure, breaking the rock.
  • **Biological Activity:** Plant roots growing into cracks, burrowing animals, and lichen/moss growth can physically break apart rocks.

How Chemical Weathering Happens:

Chemical weathering involves chemical reactions that alter the mineral composition of rocks, weakening and breaking them down. Key processes include:

  • **Dissolution (Carbonation):** Minerals (especially carbonates like limestone) dissolve in water, particularly acidic water (e.g., rainwater containing dissolved $CO_2$ forms carbonic acid).
  • **Oxidation:** Minerals containing iron react with oxygen in the presence of water, forming iron oxides (rust), which are weaker and crumbly.
  • **Hydrolysis:** Water reacts with minerals (especially silicates), breaking them down into new minerals and soluble components.
  • **Hydrolytic Action (Hydration):** Water molecules are added to the chemical structure of minerals, causing them to expand and weaken.

Landforms from Chemical Weathering:

  • **Karst Topography:** Characterized by features like sinkholes, caves, disappearing rivers, and underground drainage systems, primarily formed by the dissolution of soluble bedrock (limestone, gypsum, dolomite).
  • **Caves and Caverns:** Large underground chambers formed by the dissolution of rock.
  • **Stalactites and Stalagmites:** Formations within caves resulting from the precipitation of dissolved minerals.
  • **Pinnacles and Towers:** Eroded remnants in karst landscapes.

What is the dominant agent of erosion?

The dominant agent of erosion on Earth is **water**, in all its forms (rivers, streams, rainfall, ocean waves, glaciers, and groundwater). While wind and gravity also cause erosion, water is responsible for the vast majority of sediment transport and landscape modification globally.

In a stream, which section experiences the most erosion, and which section experiences the most deposition? What are the three stream sections called? Describe each briefly.

In a typical stream or river system:

  • The **upper course (headwaters)** experiences the most **erosion**.
  • The **lower course (mouth/delta)** experiences the most **deposition**.

The three stream sections are:

  1. Upper Course (Zone of Erosion/Headwaters):
    • **Description:** This is the steepest part of the stream, typically found in mountainous or hilly terrain. The water flows rapidly, often in a V-shaped valley.
    • **Activity:** Dominated by **erosion**, particularly vertical erosion (downcutting), creating features like waterfalls, rapids, and narrow gorges. Sediment transport is high, but deposition is minimal.
  2. Middle Course (Zone of Transport):
    • **Description:** The gradient becomes less steep, and the valley widens. The river begins to meander (curve).
    • **Activity:** Primarily a zone of **transport**, where eroded material from the upper course is carried downstream. Both erosion and deposition occur, but transport is dominant. Meanders become more pronounced, with erosion on the outer bends and deposition on the inner bends (point bars).
  3. Lower Course (Zone of Deposition/Mouth):
    • **Description:** The gradient is very gentle, and the river flows slowly across a wide floodplain, often near a lake or the ocean.
    • **Activity:** Dominated by **deposition** as the river loses energy. Features like floodplains, natural levees, oxbow lakes, and deltas are formed here. Sediment is laid down, building up the land.

What types of landforms result from erosion and deposition by water, wave, wind, mass wasting and/or glaciers? What events and/or characteristics can lead to landslides and mudslide (mass wasting events)?

Landforms from Erosion and Deposition by Various Agents:

  • Water (Fluvial Processes - Rivers/Streams):
    • **Erosion:** V-shaped valleys, canyons, gorges, waterfalls, rapids, meanders (outer bend).
    • **Deposition:** Floodplains, natural levees, point bars (inner bend of meanders), oxbow lakes, deltas, alluvial fans.
  • Waves (Coastal Processes):
    • **Erosion:** Sea cliffs, sea caves, sea arches, sea stacks, wave-cut platforms.
    • **Deposition:** Beaches, spits, barrier islands, tombolos, sandbars.
  • Wind (Aeolian Processes):
    • **Erosion:** Deflation hollows, ventifacts (wind-faceted rocks), desert pavement, yardangs.
    • **Deposition:** Sand dunes (various types: crescent, linear, star), loess deposits (fine silt).
  • Mass Wasting (Gravity):
    • **Erosion/Deposition (Directly):** Talus slopes (debris at base of cliffs), landslides (scarps at top, debris at bottom), mudflow levees.
  • Glaciers (Glacial Processes):
    • **Erosion:** U-shaped valleys, cirques, arêtes, horns, fjords, striations (scratches on rock), roches moutonnées.
    • **Deposition:** Moraines (terminal, lateral, medial, ground), drumlins, eskers, kames, outwash plains, till.

Events and/or Characteristics Leading to Landslides and Mudslides (Mass Wasting Events):

  • **Steep Slopes:** Gravity is a primary driver; steeper slopes are inherently less stable.
  • **Excessive Water/Rainfall:**
    • **Heavy Rainfall:** Saturates soil, increases its weight, and reduces cohesion between particles, acting as a lubricant. This is a major trigger for mudslides and debris flows.
    • **Snowmelt:** Rapid melting of snow can have a similar effect.
  • **Earthquakes:** Seismic shaking can destabilize slopes and trigger rapid mass movements.
  • **Volcanic Eruptions:** Can cause lahars (volcanic mudflows) or destabilize volcanic slopes.
  • **Undercutting of Slopes:** Erosion by rivers or waves at the base of a slope removes support, making it prone to collapse.
  • **Weak or Unstable Geologic Materials:** Slopes composed of loose sediment, clay-rich soils (which become slippery when wet), or fractured rock are more susceptible.
  • **Lack of Vegetation:** Plant roots help bind soil together. Deforestation or wildfires can remove this protective cover, increasing instability.
  • **Human Activities:** Construction (cutting into slopes), irrigation, mining, and improper waste disposal can destabilize slopes.
  • **Freeze-Thaw Cycles:** Repeated freezing and thawing can weaken rock and soil structures over time.

Soils

What IS soil? Differentiate between the definitions of geologists/archeologists, soil scientists, agriculturalists & horticulturalists, and civil engineers. How is it different from “dirt?”

  • What IS Soil?: **Soil** is a complex, dynamic natural body composed of mineral and organic matter, water, and air, existing on Earth's surface and supporting plant growth. It is a living system.
  • Differentiating Definitions:
    • **Geologists/Archeologists:** Often view soil as **regolith** (loose unconsolidated rock and mineral fragments that cover bedrock) or as a medium that preserves artifacts and geological history. Their focus might be on the mineral composition, stratigraphy, and age of the layers.
    • **Soil Scientists (Pedologists):** Define soil as a natural body developed from unconsolidated mineral and organic matter on the surface of the Earth, which has been subjected to and shows the effects of environmental factors (climate, organisms, relief, parent material, time). They study its formation, classification, and properties.
    • **Agriculturalists & Horticulturalists:** Primarily see soil as the **medium for plant growth**, focusing on its fertility, water-holding capacity, nutrient availability, and overall productivity for crops and gardens.
    • **Civil Engineers:** Often define soil (or "geotechnical material") as any unconsolidated material above bedrock that can be excavated without blasting. Their concern is with its **physical properties** for construction (bearing capacity, stability, drainage, compaction).
  • How is it different from “dirt?”:
    • **Dirt:** Is typically used to refer to displaced soil, especially when it's out of place (e.g., on your clothes, floor). It's a generic term for loose, earthy material that has lost its structure and biological activity.
    • **Soil:** Is a living, organized system with distinct layers (horizons), teeming with microorganisms, organic matter, water, and air, all interacting to support life. It has structure and function. "Dirt" is dead soil.

What are the two primary components that generate new soil? What types of processes are involved in soil formation from its parent materials?

  • **Two Primary Components for New Soil:**
    1. **Weathered rock/mineral fragments (Parent Material):** Provides the inorganic base.
    2. **Decomposed organic matter (Humus):** Provides the organic component, nutrients, and improves soil structure.
  • **Processes Involved in Soil Formation (Pedogenesis):**
    • **Additions:** Input of materials to the soil (e.g., organic matter from plants, dust from atmosphere, water).
    • **Losses:** Removal of materials from the soil (e.g., erosion of surface particles, leaching of dissolved minerals by water, uptake by plants, gas escape).
    • **Transformations:** Chemical or physical changes within the soil (e.g., weathering of minerals, decomposition of organic matter into humus, formation of new minerals).
    • **Translocations:** Movement of materials within the soil profile (e.g., leaching of clay or nutrients from upper to lower horizons, upward movement of salts).

List the services that soils provide for ecosystems and for people. How do they help to clean water?

Services Soils Provide for Ecosystems and People:

  • **Support Plant Growth:** Provides physical support, water, and nutrients for virtually all terrestrial plants, forming the base of most food webs.
  • **Food and Fiber Production:** Directly supports agriculture, forestry, and grazing lands.
  • **Water Regulation and Purification:** Filters and purifies water, regulates water flow (infiltration vs. runoff), and recharges groundwater.
  • **Nutrient Cycling:** Stores, transforms, and cycles essential nutrients for life (e.g., nitrogen, phosphorus, carbon).
  • **Habitat for Organisms:** Supports an immense diversity of life, including microorganisms, invertebrates, and burrowing animals.
  • **Waste Decomposition and Detoxification:** Decomposes organic waste and can break down or immobilize pollutants.
  • **Carbon Sequestration:** Stores large amounts of carbon, playing a crucial role in regulating atmospheric $CO_2$ and climate.
  • **Foundation for Infrastructure:** Provides stable ground for buildings, roads, and other human structures.
  • **Raw Materials:** Source of clay, sand, gravel, and other building materials.

How Soils Help to Clean Water:

  • **Physical Filtration:** Soil particles (sand, silt, clay) act as a physical filter, trapping suspended solids, sediment, and larger pathogens as water infiltrates through the soil profile.
  • **Chemical Adsorption:** Clay particles and organic matter in soil have charged surfaces that can bind to and remove dissolved pollutants (like heavy metals, pesticides, and excess nutrients such as phosphorus) from water through adsorption.
  • **Biological Degradation:** Soil microorganisms (bacteria, fungi) can break down organic pollutants and transform harmful chemicals into less toxic forms through metabolic processes.
  • **Nutrient Cycling:** Microbes in soil can convert excess nitrogen (e.g., from fertilizers) into nitrogen gas through denitrification, preventing it from leaching into groundwater or surface water.

What types of organisms live in soils? How many of these are microbes, and how many microbes are in a gram of soil? How much of plant matter is consumed by microbes in soils?

  • **Types of Organisms in Soils:** Soils are incredibly biodiverse, hosting a vast array of life, including:

    • **Invertebrates:** Earthworms, nematodes, mites, springtails, ants, termites, beetles, slugs, snails.
    • **Vertebrates:** Rodents, moles, snakes, and other burrowing animals.
    • **Plant Roots:** Though part of the plant, they are a vital living component within the soil.

Estimates vary, but microbes can consume **70-90% or more** of the carbon from dead plant material, breaking it down and cycling nutrients.

Describe the process by which soils, and organisms in soils, cycle nutrients. What types of medicines have been isolated from soils? Which types are Dr. Selman Waksman famous for discovering?

Nutrient Cycling in Soils (and by Soil Organisms):

Nutrient cycling in soils is a continuous process driven largely by the activity of soil organisms, particularly microbes. It involves the transformation of nutrients from organic forms (locked in dead organisms and waste) into inorganic forms that plants can absorb, and vice versa. Key steps include:

  • **Decomposition:** Decomposers (bacteria, fungi, earthworms, etc.) break down dead organic matter (plant and animal remains, waste). This process releases nutrients that were bound within organic molecules.
  • **Mineralization:** During decomposition, complex organic forms of nutrients (e.g., organic nitrogen, phosphorus) are converted into simpler inorganic forms (e.g., ammonium, phosphate) that are available for plant uptake. This is a crucial step for plant nutrition.
  • **Immobilization:** Microbes can take up available inorganic nutrients from the soil and incorporate them into their own biomass. This temporarily makes the nutrients unavailable to plants but prevents their loss from the soil system.
  • **Nitrification:** Specific bacteria convert ammonium ($NH_4^+$) to nitrite ($NO_2^-$) and then to nitrate ($NO_3^-$), a form of nitrogen highly preferred by many plants.
  • **Denitrification:** Under anaerobic (low oxygen) conditions, other bacteria convert nitrates back into nitrogen gas ($N_2$) or nitrous oxide ($N_2O$), which are released back into the atmosphere. This is a "loss" pathway but part of the larger nitrogen cycle.
  • **Fixation (e.g., Nitrogen Fixation):** Certain bacteria convert atmospheric nitrogen gas ($N_2$) into ammonia ($NH_3$), which can then be used by plants.
  • **Plant Uptake:** Plants absorb inorganic nutrients (e.g., nitrate, phosphate, potassium) from the soil water through their roots.

This continuous cycle ensures that nutrients are recycled within the ecosystem, supporting continuous life.

Types of Medicines Isolated from Soils:

Soils are a rich source of microorganisms that produce a wide array of bioactive compounds, many of which have been developed into medicines. The most famous class of medicines isolated from soils are **antibiotics**.

Dr. Selman Waksman is famous for discovering:

Dr. Selman Waksman, a microbiologist, is famous for his pioneering work in discovering numerous antibiotics from soil microorganisms, particularly from the genus *Streptomyces*. He is most notably famous for discovering:

  • **Streptomycin:** The first effective antibiotic against tuberculosis (TB), discovered in 1943. This was a groundbreaking discovery that revolutionized the treatment of TB and other bacterial infections.
  • He also discovered other important antibiotics like **Neomycin** and **Actinomycin**.

  • **Fiber:** Cotton, flax, wool (from animals), timber for paper and construction.
  • **Medicines:** Antibiotics (as discussed), other pharmaceuticals derived from soil organisms or plants grown in soil.
  • **Building Materials:** Clay (for bricks, pottery), sand, gravel, stone.
  • **Energy:** Biomass (from plants grown in soil), fossil fuels (formed in the lithosphere but from ancient organic matter that interacted with ancient soils/sediments).
  • **Water:** Filtered and stored water (groundwater) that is purified by soil.
  • **Dyes and Pigments:** From plants grown in soil.
  • **Cosmetics and Personal Care Products:** Ingredients derived from plants or minerals in soil.

Soil Formation & Factors

What is a soil profile and a soil horizon? How are they different?

  • **Soil Profile:** A **soil profile** is a vertical cross-section of the soil from the surface down to the parent rock material. It reveals the sequence of distinct layers (horizons) that have developed over time.
  • **Soil Horizon:** A **soil horizon** is a distinct layer or zone within the soil profile that differs in physical, chemical, and/or biological properties from the layers above and/or below it.
  • **Difference:** The soil profile is the *entire vertical sequence* of layers, while soil horizons are the *individual layers* that make up that profile. Think of a soil profile as a cake, and the soil horizons as the individual layers of the cake.

Which soil horizon has undecomposed organic matter? Which horizon is the most important for plant growth?

  • **Undecomposed Organic Matter:** The **O horizon** (organic horizon) has undecomposed or partially decomposed organic matter (e.g., leaf litter, twigs) on the very surface of the soil.
  • **Most Important for Plant Growth:** The **A horizon** (topsoil) is generally considered the most important for plant growth. It is rich in organic matter (humus), nutrients, and biological activity, making it the primary zone where plant roots grow and absorb resources.

List the five soil formation factors and describe how EACH influences soil formation.

The five soil formation factors (CLORPT) are:

  1. Climate (Cl):
    • **Influence:** Temperature and precipitation are key. High rainfall leads to more leaching (washing away of nutrients) and faster weathering. Warm temperatures increase the rate of chemical reactions and biological activity (decomposition). Cold, dry climates lead to slower soil formation.
  2. Organisms (O):
    • **Influence:** Plants contribute organic matter (leaves, roots) and can physically break down rocks. Microorganisms (bacteria, fungi) decompose organic matter, cycle nutrients, and contribute to soil structure. Animals (earthworms, rodents) mix and aerate the soil.
  3. Relief/Topography (R):
    • **Influence:** Slope, aspect (direction it faces), and elevation. Steep slopes lead to more erosion and thinner soils. Flat areas allow for accumulation of organic matter and deeper soil profiles. Aspect influences sun exposure and moisture.
  4. Parent Material (P):
    • **Influence:** The original geological material from which the soil forms (rock, sediment). It determines the initial mineral composition, texture (e.g., sandy vs. clayey), and nutrient content of the soil.
  5. Time (T):
    • **Influence:** The duration over which soil-forming processes have been active. Older soils generally have more developed horizons, deeper profiles, and may have experienced more leaching and weathering compared to younger soils.

Soils & Plants

What are the three important soil-water properties? Define each one briefly.

  • Infiltration Rate: The rate at which water penetrates the soil surface and moves downward. It's influenced by soil texture, structure, and organic matter content.
  • Water Holding Capacity (Field Capacity): The maximum amount of water that a soil can hold against the force of gravity after excess water has drained away. It's the water available for plants.
  • Permeability: The ease with which water (or air) can move through the soil. It relates to the size and connectivity of soil pores.

What is soil texture? Which soil particle sizes define soil texture? How does soil texture (generally) influence soil-water properties? What type of soil texture is the best for plants?

  • Soil Texture: **Soil texture** refers to the relative proportions of sand, silt, and clay particles in a soil. It's a fundamental physical property of soil that influences many other characteristics.
  • Soil Particle Sizes: Soil texture is defined by the proportion of these three particle sizes:
    • **Sand:** Largest particles (0.05 to 2.0 mm). Feels gritty.
    • **Silt:** Medium particles (0.002 to 0.05 mm). Feels smooth or floury.
    • **Clay:** Smallest particles (less than 0.002 mm). Feels sticky when wet, hard when dry.
  • Influence on Soil-Water Properties:
    • **Sandy Soils:** Have large pore spaces, leading to high infiltration rates and high permeability, but low water holding capacity (water drains quickly).
    • **Clayey Soils:** Have very small pore spaces, leading to low infiltration rates and low permeability, but high water holding capacity (water is held tightly).
    • **Silty Soils:** Have intermediate properties between sand and clay.
  • Best Soil Texture for Plants: The best type of soil texture for most plants is **loam**. Loam is a balanced mixture of sand, silt, and clay (typically around 40% sand, 40% silt, and 20% clay). This combination provides a good balance of:
    • Good drainage (from sand).
    • Good water retention (from clay and silt).
    • Good aeration (from mixed particle sizes creating varied pores).
    • Good workability.

Which other component (in addition to clay) of soil influence soil-water properties? How?

In addition to clay, **organic matter (humus)** is another crucial component of soil that significantly influences soil-water properties.

  • **How it influences:**
    • **Increases Water Holding Capacity:** Organic matter acts like a sponge, absorbing and holding a large amount of water. It can hold several times its weight in water, making it available to plants over time.
    • **Improves Infiltration and Permeability (in sandy soils):** It helps bind sand particles together, creating larger, more stable aggregates, which improves pore space and allows water to infiltrate and drain better.
    • **Improves Aeration (in clayey soils):** It helps to aggregate clay particles, preventing them from compacting too tightly, thus improving aeration and drainage in heavy soils.
    • **Reduces Evaporation:** A layer of organic matter on the surface can act as a mulch, reducing water loss from the soil surface due to evaporation.

How do soils become salty, and how does this impact plant growth? Is this a new issue?

  • How Soils Become Salty (Salinization): Soils become salty primarily through:
    • **Irrigation with Saline Water:** Using irrigation water that contains dissolved salts. When the water evaporates, the salts are left behind in the soil.
    • **Poor Drainage:** If drainage is inadequate, water tables can rise, bringing naturally occurring salts from deeper soil layers or groundwater to the surface where they accumulate as water evaporates.
    • **Arid and Semi-Arid Climates:** In dry regions, there isn't enough rainfall to leach salts out of the soil profile, leading to their accumulation over time.
    • **Coastal Areas:** Saltwater intrusion from rising sea levels or over-pumping of groundwater can introduce salts into agricultural soils.
  • Impact on Plant Growth: Soil salinization severely impacts plant growth:
    • **Osmotic Stress:** High salt concentrations in the soil make it difficult for plant roots to absorb water, even if the soil appears moist. This is because water moves from areas of higher water potential (less salty) to lower water potential (more salty), essentially drawing water *out* of plant roots or preventing uptake. This is physiological drought.
    • **Ion Toxicity:** Specific ions (like sodium, chloride, boron) can become toxic to plants at high concentrations, interfering with metabolic processes.
    • **Nutrient Imbalances:** Excess salts can interfere with the uptake of essential plant nutrients.
    • **Reduced Yields/Plant Death:** Ultimately, high salinity leads to stunted growth, wilting, leaf burn, reduced crop yields, and eventually plant death.
  • Is this a new issue?: No, soil salinization is **not a new issue**. It has been a problem for agriculture for thousands of years, dating back to ancient civilizations (e.g., Mesopotamia, ancient Egypt) that practiced irrigation in arid regions. However, the scale and rate of salinization have increased in modern times due to extensive irrigation practices, especially in vulnerable dry climates.

How do soils support plant growth?

Soils support plant growth in multiple critical ways:

  • **Physical Support:** Provides anchorage for plant roots, holding them firmly in place against wind and gravity.
  • **Water Supply:** Acts as a reservoir, storing water from precipitation and making it available to plants through capillary action in its pore spaces.
  • **Nutrient Supply:** Contains essential macro- and micronutrients in forms that plants can absorb. Soil organisms actively cycle these nutrients, making them available.
  • **Aeration:** Provides air (oxygen) in its pore spaces, which is vital for root respiration.
  • **Temperature Regulation:** Buffers soil temperature, protecting roots from extreme heat or cold.
  • **Protection from Toxins:** Can filter out or immobilize some harmful substances, protecting plant roots.
  • **Habitat for Beneficial Organisms:** Hosts a diverse community of microbes and invertebrates that contribute to nutrient cycling, disease suppression, and soil structure.

Which macronutrients do plants get from the soil, as compared to the atmosphere?

Plants obtain the following macronutrients from the **soil**:

  • **Nitrogen (N):** Primarily from soil (as nitrate $NO_3^-$ or ammonium $NH_4^+$), though atmospheric nitrogen gas ($N_2$) is fixed into usable forms by soil bacteria.
  • **Phosphorus (P):** Entirely from soil (as phosphate ions).
  • **Potassium (K):** Entirely from soil (as potassium ions).
  • **Calcium (Ca):** Entirely from soil (as calcium ions).
  • **Magnesium (Mg):** Entirely from soil (as magnesium ions).
  • **Sulfur (S):** Primarily from soil (as sulfate ions), though some atmospheric sulfur can be deposited.

From the **atmosphere**, plants primarily get:

  • **Carbon (C):** From carbon dioxide ($CO_2$) in the atmosphere.
  • **Oxygen (O):** From carbon dioxide ($CO_2$) and oxygen gas ($O_2$) in the atmosphere (and from water).
  • **Hydrogen (H):** Primarily from water ($H_2O$).

Why is cation exchange capacity of a soil important? How is it affected by soil pH?

  • **Why Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC) is Important:**
    • **Nutrient Retention:** CEC is a measure of a soil's ability to hold onto positively charged nutrient ions (cations) like potassium ($K^+$), calcium ($Ca^{2+}$), magnesium ($Mg^{2+}$), and ammonium ($NH_4^+$). Soils with high CEC can retain more of these essential nutrients, preventing them from being leached away by water and making them available for plant uptake.
    • **Fertilizer Efficiency:** High CEC soils are more efficient at holding applied fertilizers, reducing nutrient loss and improving plant nutrition.
    • **Buffering Capacity:** Soils with high CEC are more resistant to changes in pH, providing a more stable chemical environment for plant growth.

    CEC is primarily determined by the amount of clay and organic matter in the soil, as these components have negatively charged surfaces that attract and hold cations.

  • **How CEC is Affected by Soil pH:**
    • **pH and Charge:** Soil pH influences the number of negatively charged sites on clay minerals and organic matter that can hold cations.
    • **Lower pH (Acidic Soil):** In acidic soils, hydrogen ions ($H^+$) are abundant. These $H^+$ ions are also cations and can compete with nutrient cations for binding sites on soil particles. This effectively reduces the number of sites available to hold essential nutrient cations, leading to a **lower effective CEC** and increased leaching of nutrients.
    • **Higher pH (Alkaline/Neutral Soil):** As soil pH increases (becomes more neutral or alkaline), the number of negatively charged sites available to hold nutrient cations generally increases, leading to a **higher effective CEC**. This means the soil can retain more essential nutrients.

Biosphere

Biomes, Microclimates & Succession

Make sure you can reference your notes from the Beautiful Biomes lesson to identify biomes based on their characteristics (climate, latitude or altitude, general location on Earth, growing season).

*(This section requires external notes/lesson content, but here's a general reminder of key terrestrial biomes for context: Tropical Rainforest, Tropical Savanna/Grassland, Desert, Temperate Grassland, Temperate Forest (Deciduous), Boreal Forest (Taiga), Tundra, Mediterranean (Chaparral/Shrubland). Each has distinct temperature and precipitation patterns, typical latitudes/altitudes, and characteristic growing seasons.)*

What climate factors influence a region’s biome? What is the primary or most important factor?

Climate Factors Influencing a Region's Biome:

  • **Temperature:** Average annual temperature, seasonal temperature variations, and temperature extremes. It influences metabolic rates, growing seasons, and water availability (e.g., freezing).
  • **Precipitation:** Total annual precipitation, seasonal distribution of precipitation (e.g., wet/dry seasons), and form of precipitation (rain, snow). It determines water availability for plants and influences soil moisture.
  • **Sunlight:** Amount of solar radiation received, which varies with latitude and cloud cover. Essential for photosynthesis.
  • **Wind:** Can influence temperature (wind chill), moisture loss (evaporation), and physical stress on vegetation.

Primary or Most Important Factor:

The primary or most important climate factors influencing a region's biome are **temperature and precipitation**, specifically their **average annual values and seasonal patterns**. These two factors largely determine the types of plants that can grow in an area, which in turn defines the biome.

How do latitude and altitude influence biome and climate? Which biome is governed by altitude rather than latitude?

  • **How Latitude and Altitude Influence Biome and Climate:**
    • **Latitude:** As latitude increases (moving from the equator towards the poles), the average annual temperature generally decreases, and the seasonality of temperature and daylight hours increases. This leads to a general zonation of biomes from tropical (equator) to temperate to boreal to tundra/polar (poles). Precipitation patterns also shift with latitude (e.g., high near equator, low at 30 degrees, moderate at 60 degrees).
    • **Altitude:** As altitude increases (moving up a mountain), temperature generally decreases, and precipitation patterns can change (often increasing on windward slopes). This creates "altitudinal zonation" of biomes, where you can observe a sequence of biomes similar to moving from the equator to the poles, but over a much shorter vertical distance (e.g., temperate forest at base, then boreal forest, then alpine tundra, then permanent snow/ice).
  • **Which biome is governed by altitude rather than latitude?:** The **Alpine Tundra** biome is primarily governed by altitude rather than latitude. While tundra is typically found in high northern latitudes (Arctic Tundra), alpine tundra occurs at high elevations on mountains at *any* latitude (even in the tropics), where temperatures are cold and vegetation is low-growing due to harsh conditions.

What is a microclimate?

A **microclimate** is the local atmospheric conditions that differ significantly from the general climate of the surrounding area, typically over a small, localized region. These differences are often caused by variations in topography, vegetation, water bodies, or human-made structures. Examples include the cooler, moister air under a tree canopy, the warmer air on a south-facing slope, or the specific climate within a city park compared to the surrounding urban area.

What are examples of disturbances that can impact ecosystems? Describe the process of ecological succession following a disturbance. What are the steps in primary and then secondary succession? Make sure you can put the steps in order.

Examples of Disturbances that can Impact Ecosystems:

  • **Natural Disturbances:** Wildfires, floods, volcanic eruptions, earthquakes, hurricanes/tornadoes, landslides, insect outbreaks, droughts, tsunamis.
  • **Human-Caused Disturbances:** Deforestation, urbanization, agriculture, pollution, mining, dam construction, overfishing, introduction of invasive species.

Ecological Succession Following a Disturbance:

**Ecological succession** is the process of change in the species structure of an ecological community over time. It is a series of progressive changes in the composition of an ecosystem after a disturbance, leading to a more stable "climax" community (though true climax communities are rare due to ongoing disturbances).

Steps in Primary Succession:

**Primary succession** occurs in an area that is initially devoid of life and soil (e.g., newly formed volcanic rock, bare rock exposed by a retreating glacier, sand dunes). It is a very slow process.

  1. **Pioneer Species (Lichens and Mosses):** The first organisms to colonize the bare rock. They are hardy and can survive harsh conditions. They begin to break down the rock through physical and chemical weathering, and their decomposition adds initial organic matter.
  2. **Grasses and Small Herbs:** As thin soil accumulates, small, fast-growing plants like grasses and herbs can establish. Their roots further break down rock, and their decomposition adds more organic matter.
  3. **Shrubs:** Over time, shrubs begin to grow, providing more shade and contributing more organic matter, further developing the soil.
  4. **Fast-Growing Trees (Pioneer Trees):** Species like pines or aspens, which grow quickly and tolerate harsh conditions, begin to establish.
  5. **Climax Community (Mature Forest):** Eventually, slower-growing, shade-tolerant, and long-lived trees (e.g., oak, maple, hemlock) become dominant, forming a stable and diverse mature forest community.

Steps in Secondary Succession:

**Secondary succession** occurs in an area where a disturbance has removed most of the existing vegetation but the soil remains intact (e.g., after a wildfire, logging, abandoned farm field, flood). It is generally much faster than primary succession because soil is already present.

  1. **Annual Weeds/Grasses:** Fast-growing annual plants and grasses quickly colonize the disturbed area.
  2. **Perennial Herbs and Grasses:** These replace the annuals, establishing more extensive root systems and contributing more organic matter.
  3. **Shrubs and Young Trees (Pioneer Trees):** Shrubs and fast-growing, sun-loving tree species (e.g., birch, cherry, pine) begin to grow, outcompeting the smaller plants.
  4. **Intermediate/Mid-Successional Trees:** As the pioneer trees grow, they create shade, allowing shade-tolerant species to establish.
  5. **Climax Community (Mature Forest):** Similar to primary succession, the community eventually progresses towards a stable, mature forest dominated by long-lived, shade-tolerant tree species.

Aquatic Biomes/Ecosystems

How much of Earth is covered by aquatic ecosystems (biomes)?

Aquatic ecosystems (biomes) cover approximately **75%** of Earth's surface. This includes oceans, lakes, rivers, wetlands, and other water bodies.

What abiotic factors influence life in aquatic biomes? Why is each important?

Abiotic Factors Influencing Life in Aquatic Biomes (and their importance):

  • **Sunlight (Light Penetration):**
    • **Importance:** Essential for photosynthesis by aquatic producers (algae, phytoplankton, aquatic plants). Light diminishes with depth, creating distinct zones (photic vs. aphotic).
  • **Temperature:**
    • **Importance:** Influences metabolic rates of aquatic organisms, dissolved oxygen levels (cold water holds more oxygen), and stratification in lakes/oceans.
  • **Dissolved Oxygen (DO):**
    • **Importance:** Required for respiration by most aquatic animals and aerobic microorganisms. Influenced by temperature, turbulence, and photosynthetic activity.
  • **Nutrient Availability:**
    • **Importance:** Essential for primary productivity (e.g., nitrates, phosphates). Nutrient levels vary with depth, currents, and proximity to land runoff.
  • **Salinity:**
    • **Importance:** The amount of dissolved salts in water. Crucial for osmotic balance in aquatic organisms. Defines marine (high salinity), freshwater (low salinity), and estuarine (variable salinity) environments.
  • **pH:**
    • **Importance:** Affects the solubility of nutrients and the toxicity of pollutants. Most aquatic organisms have a narrow pH tolerance.
  • **Water Flow/Currents:**
    • **Importance:** In rivers/streams, determines sediment transport, oxygenation, and challenges for organisms to stay in place. In oceans, drives nutrient distribution and heat transfer.
  • **Substrate (Bottom Material):**
    • **Importance:** Type of bottom (sand, mud, rock) influences what organisms can live there (e.g., burrowing animals, rooted plants).
  • **Depth:**
    • **Importance:** Influences light penetration, temperature, pressure, and oxygen levels, creating distinct zones of life.

List and describe the different types of aquatic biomes.

Different Types of Aquatic Biomes:

  • Freshwater Biomes: Characterized by low salt concentration (typically less than 1%).
    • **Lakes and Ponds:** Standing bodies of freshwater. Lakes are generally larger and deeper, often with distinct thermal layers; ponds are shallower and may support rooted plants across their entire bottom.
    • **Rivers and Streams:** Flowing bodies of freshwater. Vary in size, speed, and sediment load from headwaters to mouth.
    • **Wetlands:** Areas where the soil is saturated with water for at least part of the year. Includes marshes (dominated by herbaceous plants), swamps (dominated by woody plants/trees), and bogs (acidic, peat-forming, often moss-dominated). Highly productive and provide vital ecosystem services.
  • Marine Biomes: Characterized by high salt concentration (average 3.5%).
    • **Oceans:** The largest biome, covering most of Earth's surface. Divided into various zones based on depth and distance from shore (see next question). Supports vast biodiversity, from microscopic plankton to whales.
    • **Coral Reefs:** Highly biodiverse underwater ecosystems built by colonies of tiny polyps. Found in warm, shallow, clear tropical waters. Extremely sensitive to temperature changes and ocean acidification.
    • **Estuaries:** Transitional zones where freshwater rivers meet saltwater oceans. Salinity is variable and fluctuates with tides and river flow. Highly productive, serving as nurseries for many marine species. Examples: salt marshes, mangrove forests.
    • **Intertidal Zone:** The area between high and low tide marks along coastlines. Organisms here must be adapted to both submerged and exposed conditions, and to wave action.

For the saltwater (ocean) biome, what are regions by distance from shore and by depth? Which have the most/least nutrients and light, and how does that influence the abundance of life?

Saltwater (Ocean) Biome Regions:

  • **Regions by Distance from Shore:**
    • **Intertidal Zone:** Area exposed at low tide and submerged at high tide. Experiences extreme fluctuations in temperature, salinity, and wave action.
    • **Neritic Zone:** Extends from the low tide mark to the edge of the continental shelf. Relatively shallow, well-lit, and nutrient-rich due to proximity to land and upwelling. **Most light and nutrients.**
    • **Oceanic Zone:** The vast open ocean beyond the continental shelf. Deep water.
  • **Regions by Depth (within the Oceanic Zone):**
    • **Photic (Sunlight) Zone (Epipelagic):** The uppermost layer where enough sunlight penetrates for photosynthesis (typically up to ~200 meters or 650 feet). **Most light.**
    • **Aphotic (Twilight/Midnight) Zone (Mesopelagic, Bathypelagic, Abyssalpelagic, Hadalpelagic):** Layers where little to no sunlight penetrates.
      • **Mesopelagic (Twilight Zone):** Some faint light, but not enough for photosynthesis.
      • **Bathypelagic (Midnight Zone):** Complete darkness.
      • **Abyssalpelagic (Abyssal Zone):** Deep ocean floor, very cold, high pressure.
      • **Hadalpelagic (Hadal Zone):** Deepest ocean trenches.

Nutrients, Light, and Abundance of Life:

  • **Most Nutrients & Light:** The **Neritic Zone** and the upper part of the **Photic Zone** (especially near coastlines or areas of upwelling) have the most light and nutrients.
    • **Influence on Life:** This combination supports the highest abundance and diversity of marine life, including vast populations of phytoplankton (primary producers), zooplankton, fish, and marine mammals, forming the base of the oceanic food web. Coral reefs are also found in these well-lit, nutrient-rich (though often oligotrophic in the water column itself, with nutrients recycled efficiently) shallow zones.
  • **Least Nutrients & Light:** The **deep oceanic (aphotic) zones** have the least light (none) and generally fewer dissolved nutrients (as nutrients sink and are not readily recycled back to the surface without upwelling).
    • **Influence on Life:** Life here is sparse and highly adapted to extreme conditions (cold, high pressure, darkness, scarcity of food). Organisms often rely on "marine snow" (dead organic matter sinking from above) or chemosynthesis (around hydrothermal vents) for energy. Biodiversity is lower, but unique and specialized species exist.

Biodiversity

How do humans benefit from biodiversity, and how have we/do we impact biodiversity?

How Humans Benefit from Biodiversity:

  • **Ecosystem Services:**
    • **Food & Water Security:** Diverse ecosystems provide a variety of food sources (crops, livestock, fish) and clean water.
    • **Pollination:** Essential for many food crops and wild plants.
    • **Pest & Disease Control:** Natural predators and diverse ecosystems can suppress pest outbreaks and disease transmission.
    • **Climate Regulation:** Forests absorb CO2; healthy oceans regulate global temperatures.
    • **Soil Health:** Microbes and invertebrates maintain soil fertility.
  • **Medicine & Pharmaceuticals:** Many drugs are derived from natural compounds found in diverse species (e.g., penicillin from fungi, aspirin from willow bark).
  • **Economic Benefits:** Supports industries like agriculture, forestry, fisheries, and ecotourism.
  • **Resilience & Adaptation:** Diverse ecosystems are more resilient to disturbances (e.g., climate change, new diseases) and provide a genetic library for adaptation.
  • **Cultural & Aesthetic Value:** Provides inspiration, recreational opportunities, and holds cultural significance.

How Humans Impact Biodiversity:

  • **Habitat Loss & Fragmentation:** Conversion of natural habitats for agriculture, urbanization, infrastructure, and resource extraction is the leading cause.
  • **Pollution:** Air, water, and soil pollution (e.g., plastics, chemicals, excess nutrients) harms species and ecosystems.
  • **Climate Change:** Alters habitats, changes species distributions, and increases extinction risk due to rising temperatures, altered precipitation, and extreme weather.
  • **Overexploitation:** Overfishing, overhunting, and unsustainable logging deplete populations.
  • **Invasive Species:** Introduction of non-native species outcompetes or preys on native species, disrupting ecosystems.
  • **Disease:** Human activities can spread diseases to vulnerable wildlife populations.

How do kelp forests off the coast of the United Kingdom benefit this ocean ecosystem?

Kelp forests off the coast of the United Kingdom (and globally) provide numerous benefits to the ocean ecosystem:

  • **Habitat & Shelter:** They create complex, three-dimensional underwater "forests" that provide crucial habitat, shelter, and nursery grounds for a vast array of marine species, including fish, shellfish, and invertebrates.
  • **Food Source:** Kelp itself forms the base of a food web, directly consumed by some herbivores, and its decaying matter (detritus) provides food for many detritivores.
  • **Coastal Protection:** The dense kelp beds help to absorb wave energy, reducing coastal erosion and protecting shorelines.
  • **Oxygen Production:** Like all photosynthetic organisms, kelp produces oxygen as a byproduct, contributing to the dissolved oxygen levels in the water.
  • **Carbon Sequestration:** Kelp forests are highly productive and absorb large amounts of carbon dioxide from the water during photosynthesis, playing a role in carbon cycling and potentially mitigating ocean acidification locally.
  • **Water Filtration:** They can help filter suspended particles from the water, improving water clarity.
  • **Nutrient Cycling:** They absorb nutrients from the water column and contribute to nutrient cycling within the ecosystem.

What are the three main categories of biodiversity? Define each briefly.

The three main categories of biodiversity are:

  • Genetic Diversity:
    • **Definition:** The variation of genes within a single species. It refers to the differences in DNA among individuals of the same species.
    • **Importance:** Allows populations to adapt to changing environments (e.g., disease resistance, climate shifts). Higher genetic diversity means a species is more resilient.
  • Species Diversity:
    • **Definition:** The variety of different species within a given area or ecosystem. It includes both the number of species (species richness) and the relative abundance of each species (species evenness).
    • **Importance:** Contributes to ecosystem stability, productivity, and resilience. More species often mean more complex food webs and more ecosystem services.
  • Ecosystem Diversity:
    • **Definition:** The variety of different ecosystems (e.g., forests, deserts, wetlands, coral reefs) within a larger geographical region or across the planet. It encompasses the variety of habitats, communities, and ecological processes.
    • **Importance:** Provides a wide range of ecosystem services and supports the overall health of the planet. Loss of ecosystem diversity means loss of unique habitats and the services they provide.

What is the Sixth Mass Extinction event? How fast is Earth losing mammals, amphibians, reptiles, and insects as compared to the background rate?

  • **Sixth Mass Extinction Event:** The **Sixth Mass Extinction event (or Holocene extinction)** refers to the ongoing extinction event driven primarily by human activities since the Industrial Revolution. Unlike previous mass extinctions caused by natural catastrophic events, this one is characterized by an unprecedented rate of species loss due to habitat destruction, pollution, climate change, overexploitation, and invasive species.
  • **Rate of Loss Compared to Background Rate:** Earth is currently losing species at a rate **100 to 1,000 times higher** than the natural background extinction rate (the rate at which species go extinct naturally over geological time).
    • **Mammals, Amphibians, Reptiles, and Insects:** While exact numbers constantly update with new research, these groups are experiencing significant and alarming rates of decline. Amphibians are particularly vulnerable, with a very high percentage of species threatened. Insect populations are also showing widespread declines, which is concerning due to their critical roles in ecosystems (pollination, decomposition, food webs). The rate of loss for these groups is far exceeding the background rate, indicating a crisis.

What is the IUCN Red List, and who uses it? For what? Describe what information is most widely cataloged on this list (i.e., which kingdom of life, locations, users).

  • **IUCN Red List:** The **IUCN Red List of Threatened Species™** is the world's most comprehensive inventory of the global conservation status of biological species. It uses a set of precise criteria to evaluate the extinction risk of thousands of species and subspecies.
  • **Who uses it and for what?:**
    • **Users:** It is used by governments, wildlife agencies, conservation organizations, scientists, educators, and the general public.
    • **Purpose:** To inform and catalyze action for biodiversity conservation. It helps to:
      • Identify species at risk of extinction.
      • Guide conservation efforts and resource allocation.
      • Influence policy decisions (e.g., protected areas, trade regulations).
      • Raise public awareness about biodiversity loss.
  • **Information Most Widely Cataloged:**
    • **Kingdom of Life:** While it aims to cover all life, the most widely and comprehensively cataloged groups are **animals** (especially vertebrates like mammals, birds, amphibians, reptiles, and fish) and **plants**. Fungi and microorganisms are less comprehensively assessed due to their vast numbers and complexity.
    • **Locations:** Assessments are global, but data availability and assessment efforts can vary by region. It covers species found in all terrestrial, freshwater, and marine environments.
    • **Users:** As mentioned above, a broad range of stakeholders.

Ecology, Ecosystems & Transfer of Energy

Define producer, consumer, and decomposer.

  • **Producer (Autotroph):** An organism that produces its own food, usually by photosynthesis (using sunlight) or chemosynthesis (using chemical reactions). They form the base of almost all food chains. Examples: plants, algae, some bacteria.
  • **Consumer (Heterotroph):** An organism that obtains energy by feeding on other organisms. They cannot produce their own food.
    • **Primary Consumers (Herbivores):** Eat producers (e.g., deer, rabbits).
    • **Secondary Consumers (Carnivores/Omnivores):** Eat primary consumers (e.g., wolves, some birds).
    • **Tertiary Consumers (Carnivores/Omnivores):** Eat secondary consumers (e.g., eagles, sharks).
    • **Quaternary Consumers:** Eat tertiary consumers (top predators).
  • **Decomposer (Detritivore):** An organism that breaks down dead organic matter and waste products, returning nutrients to the ecosystem. They are crucial for nutrient cycling. Examples: bacteria, fungi, earthworms.

What is a food chain, and how is it different and similar to a food web? What is a trophic level? What are the different levels in a trophic level?

  • **Food Chain:** A **food chain** is a linear sequence showing how energy is transferred from one living organism to another through feeding. It illustrates a single pathway of energy flow (e.g., grass → rabbit → fox).
  • **Food Web (Difference and Similarity):**
    • **Difference:** A **food web** consists of multiple interconnected food chains in an ecosystem. It shows the complex feeding relationships where organisms often eat and are eaten by multiple different species. It's a more realistic representation of energy flow than a simple food chain.
    • **Similarity:** Both food chains and food webs illustrate the flow of energy and nutrients through an ecosystem, showing who eats whom.
  • **Trophic Level:** A **trophic level** is the position an organism occupies in a food chain or food web. It indicates how many transfers of energy separate it from the primary producers.
  • **Different Levels in a Trophic Level:**
    • **Trophic Level 1: Primary Producers (Autotrophs):** Organisms that produce their own food (e.g., plants, algae).
    • **Trophic Level 2: Primary Consumers (Herbivores):** Organisms that feed on primary producers (e.g., rabbits, cows).
    • **Trophic Level 3: Secondary Consumers (Carnivores/Omnivores):** Organisms that feed on primary consumers (e.g., foxes, snakes that eat mice).
    • **Trophic Level 4: Tertiary Consumers (Carnivores/Omnivores):** Organisms that feed on secondary consumers (e.g., eagles that eat snakes).
    • **Trophic Level 5: Quaternary Consumers:** Organisms that feed on tertiary consumers (top predators, less common).
    • **Decomposers:** While not typically assigned a specific trophic level in the main chain, they break down dead organic matter from all trophic levels, recycling nutrients.

How does the biomass of different trophic levels compare with one another? Is there a pattern as you move from producers up through trophic levels? Does this pattern follow for population size as well?

  • **Biomass Comparison (Ecological Pyramid of Biomass):**
    • There is a general pattern: the **total biomass decreases significantly** at each successive trophic level as you move up from producers to top consumers.
    • This forms a **pyramid of biomass**, with the largest biomass at the producer level and progressively smaller biomass at higher trophic levels.
    • **Example:** A large biomass of grass supports a smaller biomass of rabbits, which in turn supports an even smaller biomass of foxes.
  • **Pattern for Population Size (Ecological Pyramid of Numbers):**
    • Generally, the **number of individual organisms also decreases** at each successive trophic level, forming a **pyramid of numbers**. There are many producers, fewer primary consumers, and even fewer secondary/tertiary consumers.
    • **Exceptions:** This pattern can be inverted in some specific cases, such as a single large tree supporting many insects (primary consumers), or a large number of parasites living on a single host. However, the biomass pyramid is almost always upright due to energy transfer inefficiencies.

How is energy transferred from one trophic level to the next? Approximately what % of energy from one trophic level gets transferred to the next? What does this mean with regard to the number of trophic levels that can exist - is there a limit?

  • **How Energy is Transferred:** Energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next when an organism **consumes** another organism from a lower trophic level. This energy is stored in the chemical bonds of organic molecules within the consumed organism.
  • **Approximately what % of energy transferred?:** On average, only about **10% of the energy** from one trophic level is transferred and incorporated into the biomass of the next trophic level. This is known as the **"10% Rule"** or **"Lindeman's Law of Trophic Efficiency."**
  • **What happens to the rest of the energy?:** The remaining 90% of the energy is lost at each transfer, primarily as:
    • **Metabolic Heat:** Lost during cellular respiration and metabolic processes to sustain life (movement, growth, reproduction).
    • **Undigested Waste:** Energy contained in uneaten or undigested parts of the organism (e.g., bones, fur, waste products) that are not assimilated. This energy is then available to decomposers.
  • **Limit on Trophic Levels:** Yes, there is a limit to the number of trophic levels that can exist in an ecosystem. Due to the significant energy loss at each transfer (the 10% rule), there is progressively less energy available at higher trophic levels.
    • Most ecosystems typically have **3 to 5 trophic levels** (producers, primary consumers, secondary consumers, tertiary consumers). It's very rare to find more than 5 because there simply isn't enough energy remaining to support a viable population of organisms at higher levels. This fundamental energy limitation explains why food chains are relatively short.

Define primary producers; describe who they are and what they do, including reactants and products of photosynthesis.

  • **Primary Producers (Autotrophs):**
    • **Definition:** Organisms that produce their own organic compounds (food) from simple inorganic substances using light or chemical energy. They are the foundation of almost all ecosystems, converting abiotic energy into usable chemical energy.
    • **Who they are:** Primarily **plants, algae, and cyanobacteria** (which perform photosynthesis). Some bacteria and archaea perform chemosynthesis (using chemical energy, often in deep-sea vents).
    • **What they do:** They capture energy from the sun (or chemicals) and convert it into chemical energy stored in organic molecules (like glucose). This process makes energy available to all other organisms in the ecosystem.
  • **Reactants and Products of Photosynthesis:**
    • **Reactants:**
      • **Carbon Dioxide ($CO_2$):** Absorbed from the atmosphere (or dissolved in water).
      • **Water ($H_2O$):** Absorbed from the soil (or surrounding water).
      • **Sunlight Energy:** Absorbed by chlorophyll.
    • **Products:**
      • **Glucose ($C_6H_{12}O_6$):** A sugar, used as food/energy for the plant and eventually for consumers.
      • **Oxygen ($O_2$):** Released into the atmosphere.
    • **Equation:** $6CO_2 + 6H_2O + \text{Sunlight Energy} \rightarrow C_6H_{12}O_6 + 6O_2$

Explain the concepts of GPP (gross primary production), NPP (or NEP, net primary or ecosystem production), and Ra (or Re or Rh). Plus: how they are related to one another (using words and equations), and how they vary with day/night in terrestrial ecosystems.

  • **GPP (Gross Primary Production):**
    • **Concept:** The total amount of organic matter (energy) produced by primary producers (autotrophs) in an ecosystem through photosynthesis (or chemosynthesis) over a given period. It's the total energy fixed from sunlight.
  • **Ra (Autotrophic Respiration) / Re (Ecosystem Respiration) / Rh (Heterotrophic Respiration):**
    • **Concept (Ra):** The amount of organic matter (energy) that primary producers use for their own metabolic processes (respiration) to grow, maintain themselves, and reproduce.
    • **Concept (Re/Rh):** Often used interchangeably with total ecosystem respiration, which includes respiration by producers (Ra) and respiration by consumers and decomposers (Rh - heterotrophic respiration).
  • **NPP (Net Primary Production) / NEP (Net Ecosystem Production):**
    • **Concept (NPP):** The amount of organic matter (energy) that remains after primary producers have used some for their own respiration (Ra). This is the energy available to consumers (herbivores) and decomposers in the ecosystem. It represents the net growth of producers.
    • **Concept (NEP):** The total amount of organic carbon accumulated in an ecosystem over a given period. It's the difference between GPP and total ecosystem respiration (Ra + Rh). If NEP is positive, the ecosystem is accumulating carbon (a carbon sink); if negative, it's losing carbon (a carbon source).
  • **How they are related (Equations):**
    • **NPP = GPP - Ra** (Net Primary Production equals Gross Primary Production minus Autotrophic Respiration)
    • **NEP = GPP - (Ra + Rh)** or **NEP = GPP - Re** (Net Ecosystem Production equals Gross Primary Production minus total Ecosystem Respiration)
  • **How they vary with day/night in terrestrial ecosystems:**
    • **Daytime:**
      • **GPP:** High, as photosynthesis is actively occurring with sunlight.
      • **Ra:** Occurs continuously.
      • **NPP:** Positive (GPP > Ra), as producers are generating more energy than they are consuming. This is when biomass accumulates.
    • **Nighttime:**
      • **GPP:** Zero, as there is no sunlight for photosynthesis.
      • **Ra:** Occurs continuously.
      • **NPP:** Negative (NPP = 0 - Ra = -Ra), as producers are only respiring and consuming their stored energy, not producing new energy.
    • **Overall:** For an ecosystem to grow, the total daytime GPP must exceed the total 24-hour respiration (Ra + Rh).

Explain how energy is transferred through a (grazer or detrital) food chain, or from one trophic level to the next.

Energy transfer through a food chain (or food web) is the process by which chemical energy stored in organic molecules is passed from one organism to another when one organism consumes another. This transfer is governed by the laws of thermodynamics, particularly the second law, which states that energy transformations are never 100% efficient, and some energy is always lost as heat.

Types of Food Chains:

  • **Grazer Food Chain:** Starts with living primary producers (e.g., plants). Energy flows from producers to herbivores (primary consumers), then to carnivores (secondary consumers), and so on.
  • **Detrital Food Chain:** Starts with dead organic matter (detritus) from all trophic levels. Energy flows from detritus to decomposers (bacteria, fungi) and detritivores (e.g., earthworms, millipedes), and then to their predators. This chain is crucial for nutrient recycling.

Process of Energy Transfer from One Trophic Level to the Next:

  1. **Primary Producers (Trophic Level 1):** Capture solar energy (photosynthesis) or chemical energy (chemosynthesis) and convert it into chemical energy stored in their biomass (e.g., glucose, cellulose). This is the initial input of energy into the ecosystem.
  2. **Primary Consumers (Trophic Level 2 - Herbivores):** Obtain energy by consuming primary producers. When a herbivore eats a plant, it ingests the chemical energy stored in the plant's tissues.
  3. **Secondary Consumers (Trophic Level 3 - Carnivores/Omnivores):** Obtain energy by consuming primary consumers. When a carnivore eats a herbivore, it acquires the energy stored in the herbivore's biomass.
  4. **Subsequent Trophic Levels:** The process continues as higher-level consumers eat lower-level consumers.

**Energy Loss at Each Transfer (The 10% Rule):**

Crucially, at each step of energy transfer from one trophic level to the next, a significant amount of energy is lost, typically around **90%**. Only about **10%** of the energy from one trophic level is actually assimilated and converted into new biomass by the organisms at the next level. The lost energy is primarily dissipated as heat through metabolic processes (respiration) and is also contained in uneaten or undigested organic matter. This inefficiency limits the number of trophic levels in an ecosystem and explains the pyramid shape of biomass and energy in ecological systems.
