Energy

Is energy created or destroyed? What happens to energy on Earth?

  • **Is energy created or destroyed?:** According to the **First Law of Thermodynamics (Law of Conservation of Energy)**, energy cannot be created or destroyed. It can only be transformed from one form to another.
  • **What happens to energy on Earth?:** Energy on Earth primarily originates from the Sun (solar radiation). This energy is:
    • **Absorbed:** By Earth's surface (land, oceans), atmosphere, and living organisms (photosynthesis).
    • **Transformed:** Converted into various forms (e.g., light to chemical energy in plants, chemical to kinetic in animals, chemical to heat in combustion).
    • **Flows:** Moves through ecosystems (e.g., food chains).
    • **Dissipated as Heat:** A significant portion of energy is ultimately transformed into heat, which is then radiated back into space. This is an inevitable consequence of energy transformations (Second Law of Thermodynamics - increasing entropy).

    So, while energy is not destroyed, it is constantly being transformed and eventually lost from the Earth system as heat radiating to space.

Define kinetic and potential energy, and be able to categorize forms of energy as one or the other.

  • **Kinetic Energy:**
    • **Definition:** The energy of **motion**. It is the energy an object possesses due to its movement.
    • **Examples:**
      • A moving car.
      • Water flowing in a river.
      • Wind blowing.
      • Heat (random motion of molecules).
      • Light (photons in motion).
      • Sound (vibrations).
  • **Potential Energy:**
    • **Definition:** Stored energy due to an object's **position or state**. It has the potential to do work.
    • **Examples:**
      • A rock at the top of a hill (gravitational potential energy).
      • Water behind a dam.
      • A stretched spring.
      • Chemical bonds in food or fossil fuels (chemical potential energy).
      • A charged battery (electrical potential energy).
      • Nuclear energy (stored in atomic nuclei).

Differentiate between renewable and nonrenewable energy sources. How did fossil fuels form? Why are they nonrenewable? What other energy source is nonrenewable?

  • **Renewable Energy Sources:**
    • **Definition:** Energy sources that are naturally replenished on a human timescale. Their supply is virtually inexhaustible or can be regenerated relatively quickly.
    • **Examples:** Solar, wind, hydropower, geothermal, biomass (when sustainably managed).
  • **Nonrenewable Energy Sources:**
    • **Definition:** Energy sources that exist in finite quantities and are consumed much faster than they are naturally formed or replenished. Once depleted, they are essentially gone on a human timescale.
    • **Examples:** Fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas), nuclear energy (from uranium).
  • **How did fossil fuels form?:** Fossil fuels formed from the remains of ancient plants and animals that were buried under layers of sediment and rock over millions of years. Subjected to intense heat and pressure deep within the Earth's crust, these organic materials slowly transformed into energy-rich hydrocarbons.
    • **Coal:** From terrestrial plant matter in ancient swamps.
    • **Oil and Natural Gas:** From marine microorganisms in ancient oceans.
  • **Why are they nonrenewable?:** They are nonrenewable because the geological processes that form them take **millions of years**, vastly exceeding the rate at which humans are extracting and consuming them. We are depleting a finite resource.
  • **What other energy source is nonrenewable?:** **Nuclear energy**, which relies on uranium as its fuel, is also a nonrenewable energy source. While uranium is relatively abundant in the Earth's crust, it is a finite resource that is not replenished on a human timescale.

Electricity

How is electricity produced in thermoelectric power plants? Are there other ways to produce electricity?

  • **How electricity is produced in thermoelectric power plants:**
    • Thermoelectric power plants (which include those powered by coal, natural gas, oil, nuclear, and some biomass) primarily generate electricity by **heating water to produce high-pressure steam**.
    • **Steps:**
      1. **Fuel Combustion/Nuclear Fission:** A fuel (e.g., coal, natural gas, uranium) is burned or undergoes nuclear fission to produce heat.
      2. **Boiler:** This heat is used to boil water, creating high-pressure steam.
      3. **Turbine:** The high-pressure steam is directed to spin a large turbine.
      4. **Generator:** The spinning turbine is connected to a generator, which converts the rotational (kinetic) energy into electrical energy through electromagnetic induction.
      5. **Condenser & Cooling:** After passing through the turbine, the steam is cooled (often using water from a nearby source) and condensed back into liquid water, which is then returned to the boiler to repeat the cycle.
  • **Are there other ways to produce electricity?:** Yes, there are many other ways to produce electricity that do not rely on generating steam:
    • **Hydroelectric Power:** Moving water (from dams) spins turbines directly.
    • **Wind Power:** Wind spins turbine blades directly.
    • **Solar Photovoltaics (PV):** Sunlight is directly converted into electricity using semiconductor materials (solar panels), without moving parts or heat engines.
    • **Geothermal Power (some types):** Hot water/steam from Earth's interior can directly spin turbines or be used in binary cycle plants.
    • **Fuel Cells:** Convert chemical energy directly into electrical energy through an electrochemical reaction.

What does it mean that electricity is a secondary energy source?

Electricity is considered a **secondary energy source** because it is not found naturally in a usable form that can be directly harvested. Instead, it must be **generated or converted from a primary energy source**. Primary energy sources are those found in nature (e.g., coal, oil, natural gas, sunlight, wind, uranium). We transform these primary sources into electricity to make them usable for a wide range of applications.

Which source of energy is most commonly used to generate electricity? Is it renewable or nonrenewable?

Globally, the source of energy most commonly used to generate electricity is **coal**. Coal is a **nonrenewable** fossil fuel.

While natural gas has rapidly increased its share, and renewables are growing, coal still holds the largest share of global electricity generation.

How has the use of natural gas to generate electricity changed in the last 20 years? How does the trend for natural gas usage compare to nuclear (over the last 20 years)?

  • **Natural Gas Use for Electricity (Last 20 Years):** The use of natural gas to generate electricity has **increased drastically and rapidly** over the last 20 years, particularly in countries like the United States. This surge is largely due to the increased availability of natural gas from hydraulic fracturing ("fracking") and its relatively lower cost compared to coal, as well as its lower carbon emissions compared to coal. It has often replaced coal as the dominant fuel for electricity generation in many developed nations.
  • **Natural Gas vs. Nuclear Trend (Last 20 Years):**
    • **Natural Gas:** Has seen a **steep increase** in its share of electricity generation.
    • **Nuclear:** Has remained relatively **stable or slightly declined** in its share of electricity generation in many countries (e.g., U.S., Western Europe) due to factors like high construction costs, safety concerns, and public opposition, despite its low operational carbon emissions. Some countries (e.g., China, India) are expanding nuclear capacity, but globally, its growth has been much slower than natural gas.

The use of oil (petroleum) to generate electricity has indeed decreased significantly, largely due to its higher cost and environmental concerns compared to coal and natural gas.

  • **Industrial Feedstock:** Used as a raw material (feedstock) in the petrochemical industry to produce plastics, synthetic fibers, fertilizers, and a vast array of other chemicals and materials.

How has the use of renewables changed globally over 50 years? Over 20 years? Did you expect that it had increased more or less in recent years?

  • **Renewables Use (Last 50 Years):** The use of renewables globally has seen a **significant increase**, particularly in the last two decades. While traditional renewables like hydropower have been used for a long time, the growth of "new renewables" (solar, wind, modern biofuels) was relatively slow for much of the late 20th century.
  • **Renewables Use (Last 20 Years):** Over the last 20 years, the use of renewables has experienced **rapid and accelerating growth**. This is due to technological advancements, falling costs (especially for solar and wind), supportive government policies, and growing concerns about climate change and energy security. Solar and wind power, in particular, have seen exponential growth.
  • **Did you expect it to increase more or less in recent years?:** Given the urgency of climate change and the widespread discussion about transitioning to clean energy, many might **expect it to have increased even more rapidly** than it has, or to have already largely replaced fossil fuels. However, while growth has been impressive, renewables still make up a smaller portion of total global energy consumption compared to fossil fuels, and the overall energy demand continues to rise. So, while the *rate* of increase is high, the *absolute contribution* is still catching up.

Energy and Social Justice

Is more or less energy being consumed globally as population increases? Are more or less people able to access reliable energy as population grows? What energy sources are being used to meet increasing demand?

  • **Energy Consumption & Population:** **More energy is being consumed globally** as the population increases. This is a direct correlation, as more people require more energy for domestic use, food production, transportation, and economic activities.
  • **Access to Reliable Energy & Population Growth:** **More people are gaining access to reliable energy** as the global population grows, but significant disparities remain. While hundreds of millions have gained access to electricity in recent decades, there are still hundreds of millions (primarily in Sub-Saharan Africa and parts of Asia) who lack access to basic electricity and clean cooking fuels. The goal of universal energy access is still far from met.
  • **Energy Sources Meeting Increasing Demand:** The increasing global energy demand is being met by a mix of sources:
    • **Fossil Fuels:** Still the dominant source, with natural gas seeing significant growth, and coal remaining high, especially in rapidly developing economies.
    • **Renewables:** Experiencing the fastest growth rate, particularly solar and wind power, but also hydropower and some biomass. They are contributing an increasing share to new energy demand.
    • **Nuclear Energy:** Stable or slowly growing in some regions.

    The challenge is to meet this increasing demand primarily with low-carbon and renewable sources to mitigate climate change.

Energy Consumption

List the different end use sectors (for energy) and describe each. What are the primary sources of energy that power each end-use sector?

Different End-Use Sectors for Energy:

  1. **Industrial Sector:**
    • **Description:** Energy consumed by manufacturing, mining, agriculture, and construction. Used for process heating, motor drives, and facility heating/cooling.
    • **Primary Sources:** Natural gas, electricity (often generated from fossil fuels or nuclear), coal, petroleum products, and some biomass.
  2. **Transportation Sector:**
    • **Description:** Energy used for moving people and goods by road, rail, air, and water.
    • **Primary Sources:** Almost exclusively **petroleum products** (gasoline, diesel, jet fuel). Some biofuels and electricity (for electric vehicles/trains).
  3. **Residential Sector:**
    • **Description:** Energy consumed in homes for heating, cooling, lighting, appliances, and electronics.
    • **Primary Sources:** Electricity (from the grid), natural gas, heating oil, propane, and some biomass (wood).
  4. **Commercial Sector:**
    • **Description:** Energy consumed by businesses, offices, stores, restaurants, hotels, hospitals, and other institutional buildings.
    • **Primary Sources:** Electricity (from the grid), natural gas, and some petroleum products.

How has global energy consumption changed since 1800? What was the original, primary source of energy in 1800? Which source became popular from 1850 to 1900?

  • **Global Energy Consumption Since 1800:** Global energy consumption has increased **exponentially and dramatically** since 1800. This increase is directly linked to the Industrial Revolution, population growth, and economic development. Before 1800, energy consumption was relatively low and stable.
  • **Original, Primary Source in 1800:** The original, primary source of energy in 1800 was predominantly **biomass** (wood, charcoal, animal waste) for heating, cooking, and some early industrial processes, along with human and animal labor, and some hydropower/wind power.
  • **Source Popular from 1850 to 1900:** **Coal** became increasingly popular and the dominant energy source from 1850 to 1900. It fueled the steam engines, factories, and railways of the Industrial Revolution, replacing biomass as the primary energy source.

How has the net amount of energy consumed in the form of coal, oil and natural gas changed from 2000 to 2019, as renewables have gained more footing in our global energy profile?

From 2000 to 2019, even as renewables gained more footing, the **net amount of energy consumed in the form of coal, oil, and natural gas has generally increased globally** (though with varying trends for each fossil fuel).

  • **Overall Fossil Fuels:** Total global fossil fuel consumption continued to rise, driven by increasing energy demand, especially from rapidly developing economies.
  • **Natural Gas:** Saw a significant increase in consumption, often replacing coal in electricity generation due to its lower carbon emissions and increased availability.
  • **Oil:** Consumption generally increased, primarily driven by growth in the transportation sector.
  • **Coal:** While its share in some developed countries (e.g., U.S., EU) decreased, global coal consumption, particularly in China and India, continued to rise for much of this period, only showing signs of plateauing or slight decline towards the end of the period.

The growth in renewables has been substantial in percentage terms, but the overall increase in global energy demand has been so large that it has absorbed much of the new renewable capacity, meaning fossil fuel consumption has largely continued to grow in absolute terms, rather than being significantly displaced globally in this period.

Which renewable energy source provides the most energy globally? Reminder, renewables include solar, wind, modern biofuels and hydropower. How has the increase in renewable energy compared with the use of fossil fuels over the same period of time?

  • **Most Energy from Renewables Globally:** **Hydropower** (hydroelectricity) currently provides the most energy globally among renewable sources. It has been a major source of electricity for decades.
  • **Increase in Renewable Energy vs. Fossil Fuels (Same Period):**
    • **Renewable Energy:** Has seen the **fastest percentage growth rate** over the past decade or two, particularly solar and wind.
    • **Fossil Fuels:** Despite the rapid growth of renewables, the **absolute increase in fossil fuel consumption** (especially natural gas and oil) has often been larger than the absolute increase in renewable energy over the same period. This means that while renewables are growing quickly, they are not yet displacing fossil fuels fast enough to reduce overall fossil fuel consumption globally, as total energy demand continues to rise. Fossil fuels still account for the vast majority of global energy supply.

How was fossil fuel consumption impacted by the Covid-19 pandemic?

The Covid-19 pandemic in 2020 led to a **significant, but temporary, decrease** in global fossil fuel consumption. This impact was primarily due to:

  • **Lockdowns and Travel Restrictions:** Reduced transportation (less driving, flying, shipping) led to a sharp drop in demand for oil (gasoline, jet fuel).
  • **Economic Slowdown:** Industrial activity decreased, leading to lower demand for coal and natural gas in some sectors.

However, as economies reopened and activities resumed, fossil fuel consumption largely **rebounded** in 2021 and subsequent years, often returning to or exceeding pre-pandemic levels, demonstrating the continued reliance on these energy sources.

Explain what we use fossil fuels FOR.

We use fossil fuels for a wide array of purposes that underpin modern society:

  • **Electricity Generation:** Burning coal, natural gas, and oil (historically more so) in power plants to produce electricity for homes, businesses, and industry.
  • **Transportation:** Refining petroleum into gasoline for cars, diesel for trucks and ships, and jet fuel for airplanes. This is the largest use of oil.
  • **Industrial Processes:** Providing heat and power for manufacturing (e.g., steel, cement, chemicals), and as a feedstock (raw material) for producing plastics, fertilizers, pharmaceuticals, and countless other petrochemical products.

  • **Cooking:** Natural gas is widely used for cooking in homes and restaurants.
  • **Agriculture:** Used to power farm machinery, produce fertilizers (from natural gas), and transport food.

Nuclear Energy

How is electricity generated by the nuclear fission process? List the steps.

Electricity is generated by the nuclear fission process in a nuclear power plant through the following steps:

  1. **Nuclear Fission:** In the reactor core, uranium-235 (or plutonium) atoms are split when struck by a neutron. This process, called nuclear fission, releases a tremendous amount of heat energy and more neutrons.
  2. **Chain Reaction:** The released neutrons strike other uranium atoms, causing a controlled chain reaction that continuously generates heat. Control rods (made of materials like cadmium or boron) are inserted or withdrawn from the reactor core to absorb neutrons and regulate the rate of the chain reaction.
  3. **Heat Transfer (Boiler/Heat Exchanger):** The heat generated by fission is used to heat water, typically in a closed loop, producing high-pressure steam. In some designs, this steam directly drives the turbine; in others, it heats a secondary loop of water to create steam (to prevent radioactive steam from reaching the turbine).
  4. **Turbine:** The high-pressure steam is directed to spin a large turbine.
  5. **Generator:** The spinning turbine is connected to a generator, which converts the mechanical (kinetic) energy of the spinning turbine into electrical energy.
  6. **Condenser & Cooling:** After passing through the turbine, the steam is cooled (condensed) back into liquid water, often using cooling water from a nearby source (river, lake, ocean) or a cooling tower, and then returned to the reactor to be heated again.

Is nuclear energy renewable or nonrenewable? Why? What does it mean that nuclear is an alternative energy source? Does it emit greenhouse gases?

  • **Renewable or Nonrenewable?:** Nuclear energy is considered a **nonrenewable** energy source.
    • **Why:** Because its fuel, uranium, is a finite resource mined from the Earth's crust. While there are substantial reserves, they are not replenished on a human timescale.
  • **Alternative Energy Source:** Nuclear energy is often referred to as an **alternative energy source** because it is an alternative to fossil fuels. It is a non-fossil fuel energy source that can generate large amounts of electricity without directly burning carbon-based fuels.
  • **Does it emit greenhouse gases?:** During its operation, nuclear power plants **do not emit greenhouse gases** (like $CO_2$, methane, $N_2O$) from the fission process. However, greenhouse gas emissions are associated with the entire nuclear fuel cycle, including uranium mining, processing, plant construction, and waste disposal, but these emissions are significantly lower per unit of electricity generated compared to fossil fuels.

Describe the advantages of nuclear energy. How many lives has it saved by preventing air pollution from fossil fuel burning facilities? How does it rank in deaths per amount of energy produced?

  • **Advantages of Nuclear Energy:**
    • **Low Carbon Emissions (during operation):** Does not produce greenhouse gases during electricity generation, making it a crucial tool for climate change mitigation.
    • **High Power Output:** Nuclear power plants can generate a large amount of electricity from a small amount of fuel, operating continuously (high capacity factor).
    • **Reliable & Baseload Power:** Provides a stable, consistent supply of electricity, unlike intermittent renewables (solar, wind).
    • **Small Land Footprint:** Requires relatively little land area compared to many renewable energy facilities.
    • **Reduced Air Pollution:** Avoids the air pollutants (particulate matter, $SO_2$, $NO_x$) associated with fossil fuel combustion, improving air quality and public health.
    • **Energy Security:** Reduces reliance on imported fossil fuels.
  • **Lives Saved by Preventing Air Pollution:** Studies (e.g., by NASA's James Hansen) have estimated that nuclear power has saved **millions of lives** (e.g., 1.8 million lives between 1971-2009, and potentially more since) by displacing fossil fuel electricity generation, thereby preventing deaths from air pollution.
  • **Deaths per Amount of Energy Produced:** Nuclear energy ranks as one of the **safest major energy sources** in terms of deaths per amount of energy produced. It has a significantly lower death rate (including accidents and pollution-related deaths) than coal, oil, natural gas, and even some renewables like hydropower (due to dam failures).

Where is the waste from nuclear energy stored, and how does this compare with waste from fossil fuel combustion processes?

  • **Where Nuclear Waste is Stored:**
    • **Spent Nuclear Fuel:** The highly radioactive waste (spent fuel rods) from nuclear power plants is initially stored on-site at the power plants, typically in **spent fuel pools** (large water-filled tanks that cool the fuel and shield radiation) or in **dry cask storage** (massive concrete and steel containers).
    • **Long-Term Disposal:** There is currently no permanent, deep geological repository for high-level nuclear waste in operation in the U.S. (Yucca Mountain was proposed but halted). Globally, a few countries are developing or operating deep geological repositories (e.g., Finland, Sweden) which aim to safely contain the waste for tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands of years.
  • **Comparison with Waste from Fossil Fuel Combustion Processes:**
    • **Volume:** Nuclear waste is very **small in volume** but highly radioactive. The amount of high-level waste produced by a nuclear plant is tiny compared to the waste from a fossil fuel plant generating the same amount of electricity.
    • **Toxicity/Hazard:** Nuclear waste is highly radioactive and remains hazardous for extremely long periods, requiring secure, long-term containment.
    • **Fossil Fuel Waste:** Fossil fuel combustion produces vast quantities of waste, primarily:
      • **Air Pollutants:** Greenhouse gases ($CO_2$, methane), particulate matter, $SO_2$, $NO_x$, heavy metals (e.g., mercury, lead), which are released directly into the atmosphere.
      • **Ash:** Coal combustion produces massive amounts of solid ash (fly ash and bottom ash), which can contain heavy metals and radioactive elements. This ash often requires disposal in landfills or ash ponds, which can pose risks of leaching into water.

    The key difference is that nuclear waste is highly concentrated and contained, making its management a challenge of long-term storage security. Fossil fuel waste is largely dispersed into the atmosphere or generated in massive volumes of solid waste, posing different, but often larger, environmental and health challenges.

For how long is spent nuclear waste still radioactive? Does it have any dangerous uses?

  • **How long is spent nuclear waste still radioactive?:** Spent nuclear waste remains radioactive for an extremely long time. Its radioactivity decays over tens of thousands to **hundreds of thousands of years**, depending on the specific isotopes present. Some radioactive isotopes have half-lives of hundreds of thousands to millions of years, meaning they will remain hazardous for geological timescales.
  • **Does it have any dangerous uses?:** Yes, spent nuclear waste contains fissile materials (like plutonium) that could potentially be reprocessed and used to create **nuclear weapons**. This is a major proliferation concern and why the security and international oversight of nuclear materials are extremely strict.

Renewable Energy (from the Virtual Tour)

List and describe the different sources of renewable energy. How does each work? Which ones use a turbogenerator to initiate the flow of electricity? Are any of these energy sources a good replacement for portable fuels for airplanes and other vehicles?

Different Sources of Renewable Energy:

  • **Solar Energy:**
    • **How it works:**
      • **Photovoltaic (PV) Cells:** Convert sunlight directly into electricity using semiconductor materials (no moving parts, no heat engine).
      • **Concentrated Solar Power (CSP):** Uses mirrors to focus sunlight to heat a fluid, which then creates steam to drive a turbogenerator.
    • **Turbogenerator used?:** Only in **Concentrated Solar Power (CSP)** plants. Not in PV.
    • **Replacement for Portable Fuels?:** Not directly. Solar electricity can charge electric vehicles, but direct solar fuel for airplanes is not currently feasible. Biofuels (derived from biomass) are a potential liquid fuel replacement.
  • **Wind Energy:**
    • **How it works:** Wind turbines capture the kinetic energy of wind, causing their blades to rotate. This rotational energy is then converted into electricity by a generator.
    • **Turbogenerator used?:** Yes, the spinning blades are connected to a generator (though sometimes the term "turbogenerator" is more associated with steam turbines, the principle of converting mechanical rotation to electricity is the same).
    • **Replacement for Portable Fuels?:** Not directly. Wind electricity can power electric vehicles. Hydrogen produced via electrolysis using wind power could be a future fuel.
  • **Hydropower (Hydroelectric Energy):**
    • **How it works:** Uses the kinetic energy of flowing or falling water (often from dams or run-of-river systems) to spin turbines, which then drive generators to produce electricity.
    • **Turbogenerator used?:** Yes, water-driven turbines are directly connected to generators.
    • **Replacement for Portable Fuels?:** No, it generates electricity for the grid.
  • **Geothermal Energy:**
    • **How it works:** Taps into the heat from Earth's interior. In some plants, steam or hot water from underground reservoirs directly spins turbines. In others (binary cycle), hot geothermal fluid heats another fluid with a lower boiling point, which then vaporizes to spin a turbine.
    • **Turbogenerator used?:** Yes, in most conventional geothermal power plants.
    • **Replacement for Portable Fuels?:** No, it generates electricity for the grid.
  • **Biomass Energy:**
    • **How it works:** Involves burning organic matter (e.g., wood, agricultural waste, dedicated energy crops) to produce heat, which can then generate steam to drive a turbogenerator for electricity. Can also be converted into liquid biofuels (ethanol, biodiesel) or biogas.
    • **Turbogenerator used?:** Yes, for electricity generation (similar to fossil fuel thermal plants).
    • **Replacement for Portable Fuels?:** Yes, **biofuels (like ethanol and biodiesel)** are direct replacements for gasoline and diesel, and research is ongoing for sustainable aviation fuels from biomass.
  • **Ocean Energy (Tidal, Wave, Ocean Thermal):**
    • **How it works:** Utilizes the energy from ocean tides, waves, or temperature differences in ocean water to drive turbines and generate electricity.
    • **Turbogenerator used?:** Yes, in most designs.
    • **Replacement for Portable Fuels?:** No, it generates electricity for the grid.

What are the main advantages and disadvantages of each? Yes, even renewable energy has an environmental cost…

Main Advantages and Disadvantages of Renewable Energy Sources:

  • **Solar Energy (PV & CSP):**
    • **Advantages:** Abundant, widely available (daylight), no greenhouse gas emissions during operation, decreasing costs, modular (PV).
    • **Disadvantages:** Intermittent (only when sun shines), requires energy storage, land use for large-scale farms, manufacturing of panels can be energy-intensive and involve hazardous materials, disposal of old panels.
  • **Wind Energy:**
    • **Advantages:** Abundant in windy areas, no greenhouse gas emissions during operation, decreasing costs, relatively small land footprint per unit of energy (for the turbine itself).
    • **Disadvantages:** Intermittent (only when wind blows), requires energy storage, visual impact, noise pollution, potential impact on birds/bats, manufacturing/disposal of large turbine components.
  • **Hydropower:**
    • **Advantages:** Reliable, dispatchable (can be turned on/off quickly), no greenhouse gas emissions during operation, flood control, water supply benefits.
    • **Disadvantages:** High initial construction costs, significant environmental and social impacts (habitat destruction, displacement of communities, altered river ecosystems, sediment trapping, potential methane emissions from reservoirs in some climates), limited suitable sites.
  • **Geothermal Energy:**
    • **Advantages:** Constant and reliable (baseload power), low greenhouse gas emissions during operation, small land footprint.
    • **Disadvantages:** Geographically limited to areas with geothermal activity, potential for induced seismicity, release of some gases (e.g., hydrogen sulfide), high drilling costs, potential for groundwater contamination.
  • **Biomass Energy:**
    • **Advantages:** Can be a continuous source (baseload), uses organic waste, can produce liquid fuels.
    • **Disadvantages:** Can produce air pollutants (particulate matter, $NO_x$) when burned, competition with food crops for land (for dedicated energy crops), potential for unsustainable harvesting (deforestation), can be carbon-intensive if not managed sustainably, land use impacts.
  • **Ocean Energy:**
    • **Advantages:** Predictable (tides), vast potential, low greenhouse gas emissions during operation.
    • **Disadvantages:** High installation costs, limited suitable sites, potential impact on marine ecosystems, technological immaturity for widespread deployment.
