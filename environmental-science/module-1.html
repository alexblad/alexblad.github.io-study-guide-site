Environmental Justice

Define the term/concept of environmental justice.

Environmental justice is the fair treatment and meaningful involvement of all people regardless of race, color, national origin, or income, with respect to the development, implementation, and enforcement of environmental laws, regulations, and policies. It means that no group of people should bear a disproportionate share of the negative environmental consequences resulting from industrial, governmental, and commercial operations or policies.

Describe how environmental issues often impact low-income and disadvantaged communities disproportionately - in terms of human health, access to parks and healthy foods, and exposure to harmful pollutants.

Environmental issues frequently impact low-income and disadvantaged communities disproportionately due to a combination of historical factors, economic pressures, and systemic inequalities:

  • Human Health: These communities often live closer to polluting industries (e.g., factories, waste incinerators, chemical plants, highways), leading to higher exposure to air and water pollution. This can result in elevated rates of respiratory illnesses (asthma), cardiovascular diseases, cancers, and developmental problems, particularly in children. They may also have less access to quality healthcare to address these issues.
  • Access to Parks and Healthy Foods: Disadvantaged areas often lack sufficient green spaces, parks, and recreational areas, which are crucial for physical activity, mental well-being, and mitigating urban heat. They are also frequently "food deserts," meaning there is limited access to affordable, nutritious food options (fresh produce, whole grains) due to a scarcity of supermarkets, leading to reliance on convenience stores with processed, unhealthy foods.
  • Exposure to Harmful Pollutants: Beyond industrial pollution, these communities may face higher exposure to lead from old housing, contaminated soil, and hazardous waste sites. They might also live near agricultural areas impacted by pesticide drift or areas with poor waste management, further increasing their toxic burden. This disproportionate exposure is often linked to zoning laws, housing discrimination, and lack of political power to resist the siting of undesirable facilities.

In the video, “How America’s hottest city is trying to cool down?”... Which parts of Phoenix, AZ tended to be the hottest? Why?

In the video "How America’s hottest city is trying to cool down?" about Phoenix, AZ:

  • The parts of Phoenix that tended to be the hottest were the **densely built-up urban areas**, particularly those with a lot of **concrete, asphalt, and buildings**, and **less green space or tree cover**. These often corresponded to lower-income neighborhoods or industrial zones.
  • This is due to the **Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect**. These materials (concrete, asphalt, dark roofs) absorb and retain significantly more solar radiation than natural landscapes. They then slowly release this heat throughout the day and night, keeping urban areas much warmer than surrounding rural areas or areas with more vegetation. Lack of shade from trees further exacerbates this.

What can cities do to decrease the amount of heat retained near the surface/ground?

Cities can implement several strategies to decrease the amount of heat retained near the surface/ground (mitigate the Urban Heat Island effect):

  • **Increase Green Spaces and Tree Cover:** Planting more trees, creating parks, and incorporating green roofs provide shade and cool the air through evapotranspiration.
  • **Use Cool Pavements and Reflective Surfaces:** Utilizing lighter-colored materials for roads, sidewalks, and parking lots that reflect more sunlight rather than absorbing it.
  • **Install Cool Roofs:** Using reflective or "green" (vegetated) materials on building rooftops to reduce heat absorption.
  • **Promote Green Infrastructure:** Implementing permeable pavements, rain gardens, and bioswales that allow water to infiltrate and cool the surrounding environment.
  • **Strategic Urban Planning:** Designing city layouts that promote airflow, reduce building density in certain areas, and integrate natural cooling solutions.
  • **Efficient Building Design:** Encouraging buildings with better insulation, natural ventilation, and shading elements.
  • **Water Features:** Incorporating fountains, ponds, and other water bodies can have a localized cooling effect through evaporation.

Virtual Tour of Earth’s Spheres

For all spheres, be able to describe and identify how they interact with other spheres.

The Earth's four major spheres (Atmosphere, Hydrosphere, Lithosphere, and Biosphere) are constantly interacting in complex ways. Here's a general overview of their interactions:

  • Atmosphere (Air):
    • With Hydrosphere: Evaporation (water to atmosphere), condensation/precipitation (atmosphere to water), wind drives ocean currents.
    • With Lithosphere: Wind erodes rock, volcanic gases enter atmosphere, weathering of rocks by atmospheric gases.
    • With Biosphere: Provides oxygen for respiration, carbon dioxide for photosynthesis; plants release water vapor (transpiration); living organisms influence atmospheric composition (e.g., methane production).
  • Hydrosphere (Water):
    • With Atmosphere: Water evaporates into atmosphere, precipitates from atmosphere; ocean currents influence atmospheric temperature.
    • With Lithosphere: Water erodes and transports sediments; dissolves minerals; groundwater fills pores in rocks.
    • With Biosphere: Provides water for all life; aquatic organisms live in water; plants absorb water from soil.
  • Lithosphere (Land/Rock):
    • With Atmosphere: Volcanic eruptions release gases; weathering of rocks by atmospheric gases; dust from land enters atmosphere.
    • With Hydrosphere: Landforms shape water flow (rivers, lakes); rocks store groundwater; erosion by water shapes land.
    • With Biosphere: Provides habitat for land organisms; soil supports plant growth; decomposition of organic matter forms soil.
  • Biosphere (Life):
    • With Atmosphere: Photosynthesis (CO2 from atmosphere, O2 to atmosphere); respiration (O2 from atmosphere, CO2 to atmosphere); transpiration (water vapor to atmosphere).
    • With Hydrosphere: Aquatic life lives in water; plants and animals consume water; decomposition affects water chemistry.
    • With Lithosphere: Plants anchor soil; organisms contribute to soil formation; roots break down rocks; fossil fuels form from ancient life.

Atmosphere

Describe how air pressure changes with altitude and why.

Air pressure **decreases** with increasing altitude (distance from Earth’s surface). This is because air pressure is caused by the weight of the column of air above a given point. As you go higher, there is less air above you, and therefore less weight pressing down, resulting in lower pressure. The air also becomes less dense at higher altitudes, meaning fewer air molecules are present.

Explain how the composition of Earth’s atmosphere has changed over time, and why.

The composition of Earth’s atmosphere has changed dramatically over billions of years:

  • Early Atmosphere (Hadean Eon): Likely formed from volcanic outgassing, consisting primarily of water vapor, carbon dioxide ($CO_2$), nitrogen ($N_2$), and sulfur gases. Little to no free oxygen ($O_2$).
  • Formation of Oceans & Carbon Sinks: As Earth cooled, water vapor condensed to form oceans. $CO_2$ dissolved into these oceans, forming carbonates and being locked away in rocks, significantly reducing atmospheric $CO_2$.
  • Rise of Photosynthesis (Proterozoic Eon - ~2.5 billion years ago): The emergence of photosynthetic organisms (like cyanobacteria) began to produce free oxygen ($O_2$) as a byproduct. This was a slow process, with much of the initial oxygen reacting with iron in the oceans.
  • "Great Oxidation Event" (~2.4 billion years ago): Once iron sinks were saturated, oxygen began accumulating in the atmosphere, leading to a dramatic increase in $O_2$ levels. This event was crucial for the evolution of aerobic life.
  • Evolution of Land Plants & Modern Atmosphere: Over geological time, the evolution of land plants further increased atmospheric $O_2$ and decreased $CO_2$. Volcanic activity continued to release gases, but biological processes became dominant in shaping the atmosphere.
  • Modern Composition: Today, the atmosphere is about 78% nitrogen ($N_2$), 21% oxygen ($O_2$), and trace amounts of other gases like argon, carbon dioxide, and water vapor.

The primary drivers of these changes are **volcanic activity**, **the formation of oceans**, and most significantly, the **evolution and activity of life (especially photosynthesis and respiration)**, which fundamentally altered the balance of gases like oxygen and carbon dioxide.

Describe the major gases in Earth’s atmosphere, with an emphasis on the troposphere. What elements are taken up by life forms from the atmosphere?

The major gases in Earth's atmosphere, particularly in the **troposphere** (the lowest layer where weather occurs and life resides), are:

  • **Nitrogen ($N_2$):** Approximately 78%. It is a relatively inert gas but is essential for life, primarily through the nitrogen cycle where it is "fixed" by bacteria into usable forms.
  • **Oxygen ($O_2$):** Approximately 21%. Crucial for respiration in most living organisms and for combustion.
  • **Argon (Ar):** Approximately 0.93%. An inert noble gas.
  • **Carbon Dioxide ($CO_2$):** Approximately 0.04% (and increasing). A vital greenhouse gas, essential for photosynthesis in plants.
  • **Water Vapor ($H_2O$):** Highly variable (0-4%). A powerful greenhouse gas, crucial for the water cycle and weather.
  • **Trace Gases:** Methane ($CH_4$), Nitrous Oxide ($N_2O$), Ozone ($O_3$), etc., present in very small amounts but can have significant environmental impacts.

The elements taken up by life forms from the atmosphere are:

  • **Carbon (C):** Taken up by plants (and other photosynthetic organisms) from atmospheric carbon dioxide ($CO_2$) during photosynthesis.
  • **Oxygen (O):** Taken up by most living organisms from atmospheric oxygen ($O_2$) during respiration. Also taken up from $CO_2$ during photosynthesis.
  • **Nitrogen (N):** Atmospheric nitrogen gas ($N_2$) is taken up by certain bacteria (nitrogen-fixing bacteria) and converted into forms usable by plants, which then enter the food web.

List the features of the ionosphere.

The **ionosphere** is a region within the Earth's upper atmosphere (part of the thermosphere and extending into the mesosphere) that is characterized by ionized gases. Key features include:

  • **Ionization:** Solar radiation (especially UV and X-rays) ionizes atmospheric gases, meaning electrons are stripped from atoms and molecules, creating a region of free electrons and ions.
  • **Radio Wave Reflection:** This layer is crucial for radio communication. It reflects radio waves back to Earth, allowing signals to travel beyond the horizon. Different layers (D, E, F) within the ionosphere reflect different frequencies.
  • **Auroras:** The ionosphere is where the spectacular auroras (Northern Lights - Aurora Borealis, and Southern Lights - Aurora Australis) occur. These are caused by charged particles from the sun interacting with the ionized gases in this layer.
  • **Temperature:** While the particles themselves are very hot due to high-energy absorption, the air density is extremely low, so it would feel very cold.
  • **Altitude:** It generally extends from about 60 km (37 miles) to 1,000 km (620 miles) above the Earth's surface.

Hydrosphere

Explain how water cycles on Earth. List and describe the cycle processes (“flows”) and where water is stored/collects (“stores”). Important stores are clouds, ponds, ocean, ice sheets and glaciers, soil, plants, groundwater/aquifers. Flows include evaporation, condensation, precipitation, surface runoff, streamflow, infiltration.

The **water cycle (or hydrologic cycle)** describes the continuous movement of water on, above, and below the surface of the Earth. It involves both physical and biological processes, driven primarily by solar energy and gravity.

Key Processes ("Flows"):

  • **Evaporation:** The process where liquid water turns into water vapor and rises into the atmosphere (from oceans, lakes, rivers, etc.).
  • **Condensation:** Water vapor in the atmosphere cools and changes back into liquid water droplets or ice crystals, forming clouds.
  • **Precipitation:** Water (in the form of rain, snow, sleet, hail) falls from clouds back to Earth's surface due to gravity.
  • **Surface Runoff:** Water flows over the land surface, typically into streams, rivers, lakes, or directly into oceans, when the ground is saturated or precipitation is intense.
  • **Streamflow:** The movement of water within a river or stream channel.
  • **Infiltration:** Water seeps from the land surface down into the soil and rock layers below, becoming soil moisture or groundwater.
  • **Transpiration:** Water vapor released into the atmosphere from plants through their leaves.
  • **Evapotranspiration:** The combined process of evaporation from surfaces and transpiration from plants.

Key Storage Locations ("Stores"):

  • **Oceans:** The largest reservoir of water on Earth (saline water).
  • **Ice Sheets and Glaciers:** Large masses of frozen freshwater, primarily in polar regions and high mountains.
  • **Groundwater/Aquifers:** Water stored beneath the Earth's surface in saturated zones of soil and rock formations.
  • **Lakes and Ponds:** Bodies of standing fresh water on the surface.
  • **Rivers and Streams:** Flowing bodies of fresh water.
  • **Clouds:** Water droplets and ice crystals suspended in the atmosphere.
  • **Atmosphere:** Water vapor in the air.
  • **Soil Moisture:** Water held in the pore spaces of soil.
  • **Plants (Biological Water):** Water contained within living organisms.

What tool is NASA using to monitor amounts and quality of fresh water across the globe?

NASA uses the **GRACE (Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment)** and its follow-on mission **GRACE-FO (GRACE Follow-On)** satellites to monitor changes in Earth's gravity field, which in turn allows them to detect changes in the distribution of water mass across the globe. This includes monitoring groundwater levels, ice sheet mass, and large-scale changes in surface water, providing insights into both the *amount* and indirectly, the *quality* (by tracking large-scale water movement) of freshwater resources.

Are there oceans on other worlds/planets?

Yes, there is strong evidence and ongoing research suggesting the presence of oceans on other worlds/planets (and moons) in our solar system and beyond:

  • **Europa (Jupiter's moon):** Widely believed to have a vast subsurface ocean of liquid water beneath its icy shell, potentially twice the volume of Earth's oceans.
  • **Enceladus (Saturn's moon):** Known to have a subsurface ocean of liquid water that erupts into space as geysers from its south pole.
  • **Titan (Saturn's moon):** Has a thick atmosphere and lakes/seas of liquid methane and ethane on its surface, not water. However, it may also have a subsurface liquid water ocean.
  • **Ganymede (Jupiter's moon):** The largest moon in the solar system, thought to have multiple layers of ice and liquid water oceans beneath its surface.
  • **Pluto:** Evidence suggests it might have had or still has a subsurface ocean.
  • **Mars:** While its surface is currently dry, geological evidence strongly indicates that Mars once had significant liquid water on its surface, including ancient oceans.
  • **Exoplanets:** Scientists are increasingly discovering exoplanets (planets outside our solar system) that are in the "habitable zone" of their stars, where conditions could allow for liquid water oceans on their surface.

Lithosphere

What IS the lithosphere? What are tectonic plates?

  • Lithosphere: The **lithosphere** is the rigid, outermost shell of a terrestrial-type planet or natural satellite. On Earth, it comprises the Earth's crust and the uppermost part of the mantle. It is relatively cool and brittle.
  • Tectonic Plates: **Tectonic plates** are large, rigid slabs of the Earth's lithosphere (both continental and oceanic crust, plus the upper mantle) that fit together like pieces of a puzzle. These plates are constantly moving slowly over the Earth's semi-fluid asthenosphere (a weaker, ductile layer of the upper mantle).

Does the lithosphere (i.e. tectonic plates) move? What evidence do we have for this in Greenland and Iceland?

Yes, the lithosphere, specifically the **tectonic plates, absolutely moves**. This movement is known as plate tectonics.

Evidence in Greenland and Iceland:

  • **Mid-Atlantic Ridge:** Both Greenland and Iceland are located on or near the **Mid-Atlantic Ridge**, which is a major **divergent plate boundary**. This is where the North American Plate and the Eurasian Plate are pulling apart from each other.
  • **Iceland:** Iceland is unique because it sits directly atop the Mid-Atlantic Ridge and a mantle plume (a hot spot). This combination results in:
    • **Volcanic Activity:** Frequent volcanic eruptions as magma rises to fill the gap created by the diverging plates.
    • **Rifting and Fissures:** Visible rifts and fissures on the surface where the land is literally pulling apart. Scientists can measure the widening of these rifts over time, directly demonstrating plate movement.
    • **New Crust Formation:** New oceanic crust is continuously being formed along the ridge, pushing the existing plates apart.
  • **Greenland:** While not directly on the ridge like Iceland, Greenland is part of the North American Plate, which is moving westward away from the Eurasian Plate. Evidence of this movement is seen in the widening of the Atlantic Ocean basin over geological time, with Greenland moving along with its plate.

What are fossil fuels, and how did they form (generally speaking) in the lithosphere?

  • Fossil Fuels: **Fossil fuels** are naturally occurring carbon-rich deposits (coal, oil, and natural gas) formed from the remains of ancient plants and animals that were buried and subjected to intense heat and pressure over millions of years within the Earth's lithosphere.
  • Formation (Generally Speaking):
    1. **Accumulation of Organic Matter:** Begins with the rapid burial of large quantities of organic material (e.g., dead plants in swamps for coal; marine organisms in oceans for oil/gas) in oxygen-poor environments, preventing complete decomposition.
    2. **Burial and Compaction:** Over geological time, layers of sediment accumulate on top of this organic matter, burying it deeper and deeper. The increasing weight of overlying sediments compacts the organic material.
    3. **Heat and Pressure:** As burial continues, the organic matter is subjected to rising temperatures and pressures from the Earth's interior.
    4. **Transformation:** Under these conditions, the complex organic molecules are slowly transformed into hydrocarbons (the main components of fossil fuels).
      • **Coal:** Forms from terrestrial plant matter.
      • **Oil and Natural Gas:** Form from marine microorganisms.
    5. **Migration and Trapping:** Oil and natural gas, being less dense, can migrate through porous rock layers until they are trapped beneath impermeable rock formations, forming reservoirs.

What is the pedosphere? How thick is the pedosphere compared to the lithosphere and Earth? How do soils develop and over what time frame? How do soils benefit humans and life in general?

  • Pedosphere: The **pedosphere** is the outermost layer of the Earth that is composed of soil. It is the interface where the lithosphere, atmosphere, hydrosphere, and biosphere interact.
  • Thickness: The pedosphere is extremely thin compared to the lithosphere and the entire Earth. It typically ranges from a few centimeters to a few meters in thickness. In contrast, the lithosphere is about 100 km thick, and Earth's radius is about 6,371 km. So, the pedosphere is just a tiny skin on the Earth's surface.
  • Soil Development: Soils develop through a complex process called **pedogenesis**, which involves the interaction of five main factors:
    • **Parent Material:** The original rock or sediment from which the soil forms.
    • **Climate:** Temperature and precipitation influence weathering, organic decomposition, and water movement.
    • **Organisms:** Plants, animals, and microorganisms contribute organic matter, mix the soil, and facilitate nutrient cycling.
    • **Topography (Relief):** Slope and aspect affect drainage, erosion, and sun exposure.
    • **Time:** Soil development is a very slow process.

    Over time, these factors lead to the formation of distinct horizontal layers called **horizons** (O, A, B, C). The time frame for soil development varies widely: thin, immature soils can form in decades to centuries, while well-developed, fertile soils can take **thousands to tens of thousands of years** to form.

  • Benefits to Humans and Life: Soils are fundamental for almost all terrestrial life and provide numerous critical benefits:
    • **Food Production:** The medium for growing crops and supporting agriculture.
    • **Water Filtration and Storage:** Filters pollutants from water and stores vast amounts of water for plants and groundwater recharge.
    • **Nutrient Cycling:** Facilitates the cycling of essential nutrients (e.g., nitrogen, phosphorus) necessary for plant growth.
    • **Habitat:** Provides a home for countless organisms, from microorganisms to insects and burrowing animals.
    • **Waste Decomposition:** Decomposes organic waste, recycling nutrients.
    • **Carbon Sequestration:** Stores large amounts of carbon, playing a role in regulating Earth's climate.
    • **Foundation for Infrastructure:** Provides stable ground for buildings and roads.

Biosphere

What is the biosphere?

The **biosphere** is the sum of all ecosystems on Earth. It is the global ecological system integrating all living beings and their relationships, including their interaction with the elements of the lithosphere, hydrosphere, and atmosphere. Essentially, it is the zone of life on Earth, extending from the deepest parts of the oceans to the highest parts of the atmosphere where life exists.

How does the biosphere influence the composition of the atmosphere and the water cycle?

  • Influence on Atmosphere:
    • **Oxygen Production:** Photosynthetic organisms (plants, algae, cyanobacteria) release vast amounts of oxygen ($O_2$) into the atmosphere, which was crucial in shaping Earth's oxygen-rich atmosphere.
    • **Carbon Dioxide Regulation:** Photosynthesis removes $CO_2$ from the atmosphere, while respiration (by all living organisms) and decomposition release $CO_2$. This biological pump plays a significant role in the global carbon cycle and climate regulation.
    • **Other Gases:** Microorganisms produce gases like methane ($CH_4$) and nitrous oxide ($N_2O$), which are potent greenhouse gases.
    • **Dust/Aerosols:** Pollen, spores, and other biological particles can act as condensation nuclei, influencing cloud formation.
  • Influence on Water Cycle:
    • **Transpiration:** Plants release significant amounts of water vapor into the atmosphere through transpiration, contributing to atmospheric humidity and cloud formation. Forests, in particular, are major drivers of regional precipitation patterns.
    • **Infiltration and Runoff:** Vegetation cover influences how much precipitation infiltrates the soil versus running off the surface. Forests and grasslands typically promote infiltration, reducing surface runoff and erosion.
    • **Water Storage:** Plants and animals store water within their tissues.
    • **Cloud Formation:** Biological aerosols (e.g., from forests) can act as cloud condensation nuclei, influencing where and when clouds form.

Is the amount of vegetation the same across Earth, and if not, where is it most concentrated? Why?

No, the amount of vegetation is **not the same** across Earth. It varies dramatically depending on climate, water availability, temperature, and soil conditions.

Where it is most concentrated and why:

  • **Most Concentrated:** Vegetation is most concentrated in **tropical rainforests** (near the equator) and other **humid, warm regions** with abundant rainfall and consistent temperatures. This includes regions like the Amazon Basin, Congo Basin, and parts of Southeast Asia.
  • **Why:** These areas provide the ideal conditions for plant growth:
    • **High Solar Radiation:** Abundant sunlight for photosynthesis.
    • **Warm Temperatures:** Consistent warmth promotes rapid growth and metabolic processes.
    • **Abundant Water:** High rainfall ensures a constant supply of water for plants.
    • **Nutrient Cycling:** Rapid decomposition in warm, moist conditions quickly recycles nutrients back into the soil, supporting continuous growth.
  • **Least Concentrated:** Conversely, vegetation is sparse in deserts (lack of water), polar regions (extreme cold, limited liquid water), and very high mountain altitudes (cold, thin atmosphere, harsh conditions).

How old is life on Earth? When did microbes and when did animals come to be on Earth?

  • **How old is life on Earth?** The earliest definitive evidence for life on Earth dates back to approximately **3.8 to 3.5 billion years ago**.
  • **When did microbes and when did animals come to be on Earth?**
    • **Microbes:** Microbes (single-celled organisms like bacteria and archaea) were the **first forms of life** on Earth, appearing around **3.8 to 3.5 billion years ago**. For the vast majority of Earth's history, life was exclusively microbial.
    • **Animals:** Animals are a much more recent development. The earliest clear evidence of complex, multicellular animals appears during the **Ediacaran Period**, roughly **575-541 million years ago**. The "Cambrian Explosion," a period of rapid diversification of animal life, occurred around **541 million years ago**.

What is a mass extinction event and how many mass extinction events have occurred on Earth in the past?

  • **Mass Extinction Event:** A **mass extinction event** is a relatively sudden, widespread, and rapid decrease in the amount of life on Earth. It is characterized by a sharp increase in the rate of species extinction across many different groups of organisms and across various ecosystems globally. These events are typically caused by catastrophic environmental changes (e.g., massive volcanic eruptions, asteroid impacts, rapid climate change).
  • **How many mass extinction events have occurred?** Scientists generally recognize **five major mass extinction events** in Earth's history, often referred to as the "Big Five":
    1. **Ordovician-Silurian Extinction** (~443 million years ago)
    2. **Late Devonian Extinction** (~372 million years ago)
    3. **Permian-Triassic Extinction** (~252 million years ago) - The "Great Dying," the most severe extinction event.
    4. **Triassic-Jurassic Extinction** (~201 million years ago)
    5. **Cretaceous-Paleogene (K-Pg) Extinction** (~66 million years ago) - Led to the extinction of non-avian dinosaurs.

    Many scientists also suggest we are currently in the midst of a **sixth mass extinction event**, driven by human activities.

What is a biome? Be able to recognize the names of different biomes on Earth and match them with their relative location on Earth.

  • **Biome:** A **biome** is a large naturally occurring community of flora and fauna occupying a major habitat, e.g., forest or tundra. It is a large-scale ecological unit characterized by its dominant plant life and climate, which in turn determines the animal life present.
  • **Major Terrestrial Biomes and Relative Locations:**
    • **Tropical Rainforest:** Near the equator ($0^\circ$ latitude), high rainfall, high temperatures (e.g., Amazon Basin, Congo Basin, Southeast Asia).
    • **Tropical Savanna/Grassland:** Adjacent to tropical rainforests, distinct wet and dry seasons (e.g., large parts of Africa, Australia, South America).
    • **Desert:** Around $30^\circ$ North and South latitudes, very low precipitation, extreme temperatures (e.g., Sahara, Arabian, Atacama, Mojave).
    • **Temperate Grassland:** Mid-latitudes, moderate rainfall, hot summers, cold winters (e.g., North American Prairies, Eurasian Steppes, Argentine Pampas).
    • **Temperate Forest (Deciduous):** Mid-latitudes, distinct seasons, moderate rainfall, trees lose leaves in winter (e.g., Eastern North America, Western Europe, East Asia).
    • **Boreal Forest (Taiga):** High latitudes (e.g., $50^\circ - 70^\circ$ N), cold, long winters, coniferous trees (e.g., Canada, Russia, Scandinavia).
    • **Tundra:** Arctic regions (high northern latitudes) and high mountain tops, very cold, permafrost, low-growing vegetation (e.g., Arctic Canada, Siberia, Alaska).
    • **Mediterranean (Chaparral/Shrubland):** West coasts of continents around $30^\circ - 40^\circ$ latitude, hot dry summers, mild wet winters (e.g., Mediterranean Basin, California, parts of Chile, South Africa, Australia).
  • **Major Aquatic Biomes:**
    • **Freshwater:** Lakes, rivers, ponds, wetlands.
    • **Marine:** Oceans, coral reefs, estuaries.

What are benefits to biodiversity?

Biodiversity, the variety of life on Earth at all its levels (genes, species, ecosystems), provides numerous essential benefits:

  • **Ecosystem Services:**
    • **Pollination:** Insects and other animals pollinate crops and wild plants.
    • **Water Purification:** Wetlands and forests filter water.
    • **Soil Formation & Fertility:** Microbes and organisms contribute to healthy soil.
    • **Pest Control:** Natural predators control pest populations.
    • **Climate Regulation:** Forests absorb carbon dioxide; healthy oceans regulate temperature.
  • **Food Security:** A wider variety of crops and livestock provides resilience against diseases and climate change. Wild relatives of crops can offer genetic resistance.
  • **Medicine and Pharmaceuticals:** Many drugs and medical treatments are derived from natural compounds found in plants, animals, and microorganisms.
  • **Economic Benefits:** Ecotourism, fishing, timber, and other industries rely on healthy ecosystems and biodiversity.
  • **Resilience and Stability:** Diverse ecosystems are generally more stable and resilient to disturbances (e.g., disease outbreaks, climate shifts) because they have a wider range of species that can adapt or fill ecological roles.
  • **Aesthetic and Cultural Value:** Nature provides beauty, inspiration, recreational opportunities, and holds deep cultural and spiritual significance for many societies.
  • **Scientific Knowledge:** Studying biodiversity helps us understand life's processes, evolution, and how ecosystems function.
